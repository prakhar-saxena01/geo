# PHASE III — REGIONAL GEOGRAPHY AND POSSIBILISM

## _Early to Mid-20th Century_

### Postgraduate Lecture Notes — Research Level

---

## PREFATORY NOTE — METHODOLOGICAL ORIENTATION

Phase III presents a distinctive interpretive challenge: it is the phase most frequently dismissed in retrospective accounts of geographic intellectual history as a period of **descriptive stagnation** — the era of the endless regional monograph, the atheoretical _pays_, the formulaic survey of geology, settlement, and land use that Schaefer and the quantitative revolutionaries rightly identified as insufficient. This dismissal, while not without foundation, is intellectually inadequate.

The dismissal misses three things of enduring significance.

First, it mistakes **descriptive richness for theoretical vacuity**. The best work of Phase III — Vidal's _Tableau_, Braudel's _Mediterranean_, Sauer's _Agricultural Origins_, Hartshorne's _Nature of Geography_ — was theoretically sophisticated in ways that the quantitative revolution's subsequent spatial science was not. It grasped something real about geographic knowledge that positivist spatial science excluded: the **irreducible particularity of places**, the way specific histories, specific ecological configurations, and specific cultural traditions produced geographies that were genuinely unique and not reducible to instances of general spatial laws.

Second, it understates the **methodological achievement** of the regional tradition. The regional monograph at its best — the French _thèse régionale_, the American regional survey in the tradition of **Preston James** — constituted a form of synthetic geographic knowledge that integrated physical and human analysis across multiple scales and multiple time periods in ways that no subsequent geographic methodology has replicated. The loss of this synthetic capacity is one of the discipline's most significant intellectual costs.

Third, and most importantly for postgraduate research, it fails to recognise that Phase III's constitutive debates — between the particular and the general, between description and explanation, between the region as empirical reality and the region as analytical construct — are **still unresolved**. The emergence of **new regionalism** in economic geography in the 1990s, the return of **place** as a central geographic concept in Phase V, and the contemporary debates about **planetary urbanisation** and the dissolution of the regional concept — all of these are continuations of Phase III's unfinished theoretical business.

**Ron Johnston** and **James Sidaway's** _**Geography and Geographers: Anglo-American Human Geography since 1945**_ (7th ed., 2015) — Chapters 1–3 — provides the most careful periodisation. **Anne Buttimer's** _**Society and Milieu in the French Geographic Tradition**_ (1971) remains the definitive anglophone account of the Vidalian tradition. **Martin Kenzer's** edited volume _**Carl O. Sauer: A Tribute**_ (1987) situates Sauer within Phase III's broader intellectual context. **Geoffrey Martin's** _**All Possible Worlds**_ (4th ed., 2005, Chapters 10–14) provides the most comprehensive historical survey.

## PART I — THE INTELLECTUAL CONTEXT OF PHASE III

### 1.1 What Phase III Was Responding To

Phase III regional geography was not a spontaneous intellectual development. It was a **response to a specific theoretical crisis** — the collapse of environmental determinism as a credible scientific framework — and a **response to a specific institutional imperative** — the need to define geography's distinctive contribution to knowledge in an era of increasing academic disciplinary specialisation.

The **theoretical crisis** was clear by the 1920s: environmental determinism had failed empirically, had been exposed as ideologically loaded, and had generated increasing discomfort among geographers who recognised its scientific inadequacy. But the rejection of determinism raised an immediate question: if environment did not determine culture, what was geography's explanatory framework? What distinctive theoretical contribution could geography make that could not be made by geology, economics, sociology, or anthropology?

The **institutional imperative** was equally pressing. Geography was establishing itself in universities across North America and Western Europe — competing for resources, students, and academic prestige with more firmly established disciplines. Its intellectual identity needed to be sufficiently distinct to justify its institutional existence. The regional geography paradigm provided that identity: geography as the discipline of **areal synthesis** — the integration of physical and human knowledge about specific portions of Earth's surface — was a contribution no other discipline made.

The **political context** of Phase III must also be acknowledged. The period between the two World Wars was one in which the relationship between national territory, national culture, and national identity was politically charged in ways that gave regional geography an ideological salience beyond its academic significance. The French regional tradition's emphasis on the distinctive character of French regions — their deep historical roots, their organic relationship between landscape and way of life — was not politically innocent. As **Herman Lebovics** argues in _**True France: The Wars over Cultural Identity, 1900–1945**_ (1992), Vidalian regional geography contributed to a specifically conservative, ruralist conception of French national identity that was mobilised by right-wing political movements between the wars. The relationship between regional geographic discourse and nationalist ideology — in France, Germany, the United States, and elsewhere — deserves sustained critical attention.

---

### 1.2 The Three Intellectual Streams of Phase III

Phase III regional geography drew on three distinct intellectual streams that it combined in different ways in different national contexts:

**The Vidalian possibilist tradition** — developed in France and discussed in Phase II — provided the most theoretically sophisticated framework: the emphasis on _genre de vie_, _pays_, and _circulation_; the insistence on the historically specific character of human-environment relationships; the commitment to detailed fieldwork and empirical description; the anti-determinist theoretical stance.

**The German morphological tradition** — associated with **Otto Schlüter's** concept of the _Kulturlandschaft_ (cultural landscape) and transmitted to North America through **Carl Sauer** — provided a framework focused on the **visible, material transformation of Earth's surface** by human activity: geography as the study of what could be seen on the ground, the forms impressed by culture on the natural landscape.

**The Kantian chorological tradition** — systematised by **Alfred Hettner** in Germany and transmitted to the anglophone world through **Richard Hartshorne** — provided the most philosophically rigorous justification for regional geography: geography as the **chorological science** of areal differentiation, whose distinctive task was the study of how and why the Earth's surface varied from place to place.

The tension between these three streams — and particularly between the Vidalian-possibilist emphasis on the historically particular and the Kantian-chorological emphasis on methodological rigour — was the productive intellectual tension that drove Phase III's most significant work and generated the theoretical impasse that the quantitative revolution subsequently attempted to resolve.

## PART II — PAUL VIDAL DE LA BLACHE AND THE VIDALIAN TRADITION

### 2.1 Vidal's Intellectual Position — A Re-assessment

**Paul Vidal de la Blache** (1845–1918) — Professor at the École Normale Supérieure (1877–1898) and the Sorbonne (1898–1909); founding editor of the _**Annales de Géographie**_ (1891) — was not simply the opponent of environmental determinism. He was the architect of a **positive geographic program** that defined French geography for half a century and established the conceptual vocabulary — _genre de vie_, _pays_, _circulation_, _milieu_ — within which French regional geography operated.

The standard anglophone account of Vidal — derived primarily from **Buttimer's** _**Society and Milieu**_ (1971) and **Paul Claval's** _**Géographie Humaine et Économique Contemporaine**_ (1984) — presents him primarily as a possibilist: the geographer who argued that environment set possibilities rather than determining outcomes. This is accurate but incomplete. At the postgraduate level, Vidal's positive theoretical contributions require fuller treatment.

**Vidal's concept of geography** was stated most completely in his programmatic article _"Les Conditions Géographiques des Faits Sociaux"_ (_**Annales de Géographie**_, 1902) and in his posthumous _**Principes de Géographie Humaine**_ (_**Principles of Human Geography**_, 1922, edited by **Emmanuel de Martonne**):

> _"Geography is the science of places, not of men. It is interested in the qualities and potentialities of countries, in their situation, in their extent, in their physical constitution. It brings to the study of events the feeling of terrestrial solidarity, an awareness of the complex of natural forces which make each country a sort of individuality."_ — Vidal de la Blache, _Principles of Human Geography_ (Bingham translation, 1926), p. 6

This passage identifies several key elements of Vidal's geographic theory:

**Geography as the science of places** — the explicit definition of geography as the study of **places** (lieux, contrées, pays) rather than of human beings or physical processes per se. Geography was not a science of human populations (that was anthropology and sociology) or of physical processes (that was geology and meteorology) — it was the science of the **specific, integrated character** of particular portions of Earth's surface.

**Terrestrial solidarity** — the concept of Earth as an integrated system of mutual dependencies — directly Humboldtian. Vidal's possibilism was not an abandonment of the search for systematic relationships between environment and human life; it was a **reformulation** of that search in terms of possibilities, tendencies, and historical trajectories rather than deterministic laws.

**The individuality of places** — each pays had a character that was **sui generis** — specific to itself, not derivable from its position in a typological scheme. This was the epistemological foundation of the regional monograph tradition: the conviction that geographic understanding required engagement with the specific, not merely classification of the general.

### 2.2 The Core Concepts — Systematic Analysis

#### 2.2.1 Genre de Vie

The _**genre de vie**_ (way of life) was Vidal's most original and most theoretically productive concept — and the one that most clearly distinguished his possibilism from environmental determinism while preserving the systematic study of human-environment relationships.

The concept was developed most fully in his article _"Les Genres de Vie dans la Géographie Humaine"_ (_**Annales de Géographie**_, 1911) and in _**Principes de Géographie Humaine**_ (1922):

> _"A genre de vie constitutes a coherent system... in which the character of the environment is reflected, as in a mirror, but in the form in which it has been moulded by the group... A genre de vie has a formative influence on the men who practice it."_ — Vidal de la Blache, _Principes de Géographie Humaine_ (Bingham translation), p. 166

The _genre de vie_ had three constitutive dimensions:

**Technical-material** — the specific **ensemble of techniques** through which a community exploited its environment: agricultural tools and methods, building materials and construction techniques, textile production, food processing, transportation technologies. These techniques were not simply responses to environmental affordances — they were historically accumulated, culturally transmitted, and refined over generations through collective experimentation.

**Social-organisational** — the **social structures** through which collective life was organised: family forms, settlement patterns, seasonal rhythms of dispersal and aggregation, division of labour between genders and age groups, property relations, governance forms. These were not environmentally determined but were adapted over time to the specific technical-material ensemble and the specific environmental conditions.

**Cultural-symbolic** — the **cultural practices, beliefs, and representations** through which a community understood its relationship to its environment: religious practices associated with agricultural cycles, aesthetic traditions derived from landscape character, mythological narratives about places and their histories.

The _genre de vie_ concept was synthetic — it held together technical, social, and cultural dimensions that other disciplines (economics, sociology, anthropology) tended to separate. It was also **historical** — the _genre de vie_ of a community was not its response to current environmental conditions but the accumulated product of its historical interaction with those conditions over many generations. This gave the concept a temporal depth that environmental determinism lacked: it could explain not only current geographic patterns but the persistence of earlier adaptations into conditions where they were no longer optimal.

**The analytical power of the _genre de vie_**:

The concept allowed Vidal to explain **geographic diversity within similar environments**: two regions with similar physical conditions could develop different _genres de vie_ because they had different historical trajectories, different cultural inheritances, and different relationships to wider networks of circulation. This was the heart of the possibilist argument: cultural-historical specificity, not environmental necessity, produced geographic difference.

It also allowed him to explain **geographic resilience in changing conditions**: a _genre de vie_ developed over centuries under specific conditions would persist long after those conditions had changed — through cultural inertia, through the sunk costs of material infrastructure, through the social structures organised around it. The persistence of traditional agricultural practices in regions where they were economically suboptimal, the continuation of settlement forms originally adapted to security conditions no longer relevant — these were geographic facts explained by the _genre de vie's_ inertial properties.

**The limitations of the _genre de vie_**:

**The power dimension** — the _genre de vie_ described the practices of a community as if those practices were collectively chosen and collectively beneficial. It had no adequate account of the internal divisions — class, gender, age — within communities, or of the power relations that determined whose _genre de vie_ counted and whose labour sustained it. As **Doreen Massey** argued (_Space, Place and Gender_, 1994), the Vidalian geography of _genres de vie_ systematically obscured the gendered division of labour that structured agricultural communities: it described the **communal** _genre de vie_ from a perspective that was implicitly masculine and bourgeois.

**The colonial silence** — the _genre de vie_ concept was developed from and for the analysis of French regional geography. Its application to colonial territories — where the _genres de vie_ of indigenous communities had been violently disrupted by colonial intervention — was either absent or unreflective. Vidal's geography had nothing to say about the destruction of traditional _genres de vie_ by colonial land seizure, forced labour, and the imposition of export agriculture.

**The ahistorical tendency** — despite its emphasis on historical formation, the _genre de vie_ concept tended to present traditional ways of life as timeless — as if they had always existed and would continue to exist. It could not adequately explain the **dynamic transformation** of _genres de vie_ under industrial capitalism — the replacement of traditional agricultural communities by industrial urban ones, the global spread of capitalist market relations, the homogenisation of _genres de vie_ by mass production and mass consumption.

#### 2.2.2 Pays and Région

The _**pays**_ — the small, distinctive region defined by the coherent character produced by the long historical interaction between physical setting and human occupance — was the fundamental unit of geographic analysis in the Vidalian tradition.

The concept must be distinguished from administrative or political region. The _pays_ was not defined by administrative boundaries but by the **organic coherence** of its physical and human character: the **Beauce** was the _pays_ of the wheat-growing plain south of Paris, defined by its flat topography, its deep, fertile loess soils, its open-field agricultural system, and its distinctive large-farm settlement pattern — a coherence produced by centuries of adaptation between the agricultural community and the physical environment. The **Bocage** of Normandy was a different _pays_ — defined by its hedged fields, its dispersed farmsteads, its mixed cattle-arable agriculture, and its Celtic linguistic substrate — despite similar physical conditions in some respects.

Vidal's treatment of the _pays_ raised a question that he never fully resolved and that became the central methodological problem of Phase III: **how was the boundary of a _pays_ to be determined?** The _pays_ was defined by organic coherence — but coherence was not a property that could be measured. Different criteria (physical, agricultural, settlement, linguistic) produced different boundaries. The _pays_ was less a discoverable empirical reality than a **theoretical construction** — an analytical frame imposed on continuous geographic space by the observer.

This epistemological difficulty — the impossibility of defining regional boundaries objectively — was identified by **Hartshorne** in _The Nature of Geography_ (1939) as one of the central methodological problems of regional geography, and it was never adequately resolved. **Roger Minshull's** _**Regional Geography: Theory and Practice**_ (1967) surveyed the various approaches proposed — functional regions, formal regions, nodal regions, planning regions — without producing a satisfactory synthesis.

The _**région**_ (region) — a larger-scale unit than the _pays_ — was the framework within which multiple _pays_ were organised. Vidal's analysis of French regions — the North, the Paris Basin, the Massif Central, the Mediterranean South, Alsace-Lorraine — showed how the distinctive characters of constituent _pays_ were articulated into larger regional personalities through patterns of _circulation_ (movement, trade, cultural diffusion) and historical political formation.

---

#### 2.2.3 Circulation

**Circulation** — the movement of people, goods, ideas, and cultural practices across geographic space — was Vidal's most original and most underappreciated theoretical concept, and the one most directly anticipatory of contemporary geographic theory.

Developed in _"La Géographie Politique: À Propos des Écrits de M. Frédéric Ratzel"_ (_**Annales de Géographie**_, 1898) and elaborated in _**La France de l'Est**_ (_**France of the East**_, 1917) and _**Principes**_ (1922), the circulation concept argued that regions were not isolated entities but **nodes in networks of movement**:

> _"Circulation is the most powerful agent that we know for the opening up of barriers... it is the very condition of the existence and permanence of regions; it renews their energy and sustains their vitality."_ — Vidal de la Blache, _Principles of Human Geography_ (Bingham translation), p. 340

The circulation concept had several important theoretical implications:

**Regions as relational** — the character of a region was not simply the product of its internal physical and human conditions but of its **connections to other regions**: the trade routes that passed through it, the migrations that brought new populations and ideas, the administrative networks that connected it to wider political systems. This was a **relational** conception of region that anticipated **Massey's** progressive sense of place by 70 years.

**Scale articulation** — circulation operated across multiple scales simultaneously: the daily movement of peasants between farm and market, the seasonal transhumance of pastoralists between valley and mountain pasture, the long-distance trade routes connecting regional economies to global markets. Understanding a region required understanding its role in circulation systems at all relevant scales — not just its internal characteristics.

**The dynamics of regional change** — circulation was the mechanism through which regions were transformed. New transportation technologies (railways, roads) restructured circulation patterns and thereby transformed regional characters: regions that had been integrated through river trade were reorganised by rail connections to metropolitan markets; regions that had been relatively isolated were opened to external competition; the expansion of market circulation eroded traditional _genres de vie_ and substituted new ones oriented toward commercial rather than subsistence production.

The circulation concept was Vidal's most prophetic contribution to geographic theory. Contemporary **relational economic geography** (Coe, Dicken, Hess, Yeung), **global production networks theory**, and **actor-network approaches** to economic geography are all, in different ways, elaborations of the Vidalian insight that regions are constituted by the flows that pass through them as much as by the characteristics fixed within them.

### 2.3 The _Tableau de la Géographie de la France_ — Close Reading

_**Tableau de la Géographie de la France**_ (1903) — the introductory volume to **Ernest Lavisse's** 27-volume _**Histoire de France**_ — is simultaneously a **masterwork of geographic synthesis**, a **foundational text of French regional geography**, and a **model of geographic writing** that postgraduate students of geography should read in its entirety.

The work was described by **Lucien Febvre** as _"a book of geography in which a historian can recognise himself"_ — a formulation that captures its distinctive quality: it is geographic in its organisation around the physical and regional character of French territory, but historical in its insistence that France's geographic character was the product of a specific historical process of territorial formation, cultural sedimentation, and economic development.

**The structural logic of the _Tableau_**:

The work opens not with political France but with physical France — the geological structure, the river systems, the climatic character — but it does so not in the manner of a physical geography textbook (systematic description of each physical element) but in the manner of a **geographer thinking about integration**: how the physical elements combined, in specific regional configurations, to produce the conditions within which French historical development occurred.

The central analytical move — repeated in each regional chapter — was to show the **threefold interaction** of:

1. **The physical milieu** — the geological structure, the landforms, the hydrological network, the climatic character, the soils
2. **The historical occupance** — the successive populations that had inhabited the region, the cultural and technical traditions they had brought, the transformations they had made to the physical landscape
3. **The contemporary genre de vie** — the current agricultural system, settlement pattern, economic orientation, and cultural character that was the product of the long interaction between the physical milieu and the historical occupance

**Regional chapters of the _Tableau_**:

The regional chapters — covering the Paris Basin, Brittany, Normandy, the North, the East, the Massif Central, the Alps, the Midi, and the Mediterranean South — each constituted what Vidal called a **portrait de pays**: a geographic characterisation that captured the distinctive individuality of the region without reducing it to a type.

The chapter on **Brittany** — perhaps the most celebrated — showed how the combination of ancient, resistant crystalline rocks producing thin, acidic soils; a maritime climate of high humidity and mild temperatures; a historically Celtic linguistic and cultural substrate; and the persistence of a bocage landscape of hedged fields and dispersed farmsteads had produced a _pays_ of distinctive character: conservative, Catholic, maritimely oriented, linguistically distinct, and culturally resistant to Parisian centralism. This was not environmental determination — Brittany's physical conditions did not necessitate this human character — but it was the demonstration of how specific physical conditions, in combination with specific historical trajectories, had produced a specific regional personality.

**The _Tableau_ as geographic writing**:

**Vidal's prose style** in the _Tableau_ is as important as its analytical content. The work was written for an educated general audience — not for specialist geographers — and Vidal brought to it a descriptive precision and a literary sensitivity that made it simultaneously a geographic text and a literary one. His descriptions of landscapes — the light of the Île-de-France, the hedged enclosures of the Bocage, the limestone causses of the Massif Central — combined geographic accuracy with aesthetic attentiveness in a way that few geographic texts before or since have achieved.

As **Claval** (_La Géographie Humaine et Économique Contemporaine_, 1984) notes, this literary quality was not incidental but methodologically significant: Vidal believed that geographic understanding required **aesthetic engagement** with the specific character of places — that the geographer who had not been moved by the landscape of Provence or the coast of Brittany had not fully understood it. This was an epistemological position — a claim that affective, aesthetic engagement was a valid mode of geographic knowing — that anticipated **humanistic geography's** phenomenological turn by half a century.

### 2.4 The Annales Connection

The most significant and most productive intellectual relationship of the Vidalian tradition was its connection to the **Annales school of historical writing** — the intellectual movement founded by **Marc Bloch** (1886–1944) and **Lucien Febvre** (1878–1956) in the journal _**Annales d'Histoire Économique et Sociale**_ (founded 1929).

The Annales school was explicitly geographic in its intellectual formation: both Bloch and Febvre had studied under or alongside Vidalian geographers, and they shared the conviction that historical analysis required systematic attention to **geographic conditions** — the physical environment, the regional structure, the patterns of communication and circulation — that shaped historical processes over long time periods.

**Lucien Febvre's** _**La Terre et l'Évolution Humaine**_ (_**A Geographical Introduction to History**_, 1922) — the canonical statement of possibilism — was simultaneously a methodological contribution to historical writing: it argued that historians needed to understand the geographic conditions of the periods they studied, and that geography needed to inform historical analysis rather than being treated as static background.

**Marc Bloch's** _**Les Caractères Originaux de l'Histoire Rurale Française**_ (_**French Rural History**_, 1931) — the most important work of historical geography in the Annales tradition — used geographic methods (field system mapping, settlement pattern analysis, landscape reading) to reconstruct the agricultural history of France from the Middle Ages to the 19th century. Bloch demonstrated that the distinction between the **open-field system** (champagne — large fields without hedges, collective cultivation) and the **bocage system** (hedged fields, dispersed farmsteads, individual cultivation) was not primarily determined by physical conditions but by cultural-historical factors — the different settlement patterns and agrarian social structures brought by different waves of settlement in different regions of France. This was possibilism applied to historical geography: demonstrating the primacy of cultural-historical agency over environmental determination.

**Fernand Braudel** (1902–1985) — the dominant figure of the second generation of the Annales school — produced in _**La Méditerranée et le Monde Méditerranéen à l'Époque de Philippe II**_ (_**The Mediterranean and the Mediterranean World in the Age of Philip II**_, 2 vols., 1949; revised ed. 1966; English trans. **Siân Reynolds**, 2 vols., 1972–1973) the most ambitious and most successful integration of geographic and historical analysis in the 20th century — and one of the greatest works of geographic writing.

### 2.5 Braudel's Mediterranean — Geohistory at the Grandest Scale

**Fernand Braudel's** _Mediterranean_ deserves extended treatment at the postgraduate level because it represents the maximum achievement of Phase III's synthesising ambition and because its conceptual innovations — the _longue durée_, the geographical structure of historical time, the Mediterranean as a geographic and historical unit — remain among the most productive in the human sciences.

**The tripartite temporal structure**:

Braudel's most famous theoretical innovation was the division of historical time into three levels of duration, each corresponding to a different relationship between geographic conditions and human agency:

_**La longue durée**_ (the long duration) — the **geographic and climatic time** of mountains, plains, and seas: the time of nearly imperceptible change, within which geographic structures constrained and channelled human action over centuries and millennia. This was the time of the Mediterranean itself — the sea's climate, its coastlines, its islands and peninsulas, its alternation of plains and mountains — which changed so slowly as to constitute a near-permanent framework for human life. As Braudel wrote in his opening paragraph:

> _"I have loved the Mediterranean with passion, no doubt because I am a northerner like so many others in whose footsteps I have followed. I have joyfully dedicated long years of study to it — much more than all my youth. In return, I hope that a little of this love and a great deal of the Mediterranean's own light will illuminate these pages and that this work will be a contribution to the history of one of the greatest of human experiences."_ — Braudel, _The Mediterranean_ (Reynolds translation), vol. I, p. 17

_**La conjoncture**_ (the conjuncture) — the **social, economic, and demographic time** of cycles, trends, and medium-term rhythms: the time of price movements, population growth and decline, commercial expansion and contraction, agricultural improvement and deterioration. This was the time of 10–50 year cycles — long enough to be geographic in their effects (transforming land use, reorganising settlement patterns, restructuring trade routes) but short enough to be historically recoverable from documentary evidence.

_**L'événement**_ (the event) — the **individual and political time** of events, decisions, and biographical trajectories: the time of battles, treaties, court intrigues, and individual agency. This was the time of traditional political history — what Braudel called _l'histoire événementielle_ (event history) — which he argued was the least geographically significant dimension of historical process, the surface foam on the deeper currents of geographic and economic time.

**The geographical methodology of the _Mediterranean_**:

The first part of the _Mediterranean_ — _The Role of the Environment_ — is the most sustained exercise in Vidalian regional geography at a macroscale ever attempted. Braudel analyses:

**The mountains** — as refuges from lowland political authority, as zones of pastoral transhumance, as sources of seasonal migrant labour descending to coastal plains

**The plains and plateaux** — as sites of agricultural production, demographic concentration, and political power, but also as zones of malarial infection, agricultural insecurity, and periodic depopulation

**The climate** — the **Mediterranean climate** (warm, dry summer; mild, wet winter) as the unifying environmental framework of the Mediterranean world, determining the seasonal rhythms of agriculture, navigation, pastoral movement, and military campaigning

**The sea itself** — as the medium of connectivity, the route of commerce and cultural diffusion, the boundary of political authority, and the resource base for Mediterranean fishing and maritime economies

**The specific geographic contributions of the _Mediterranean_**:

**The sea as geographic unit** — Braudel's decision to take the Mediterranean Sea — and not any political, linguistic, or cultural unit — as the organizing framework of his analysis was a methodological choice of major significance. It demonstrated that geographic units defined by physical connectivity (sea routes, shared climate, shared biological systems) could be more analytically productive than political or cultural units for understanding historical processes.

**The historical geography of long-run change** — Braudel's _longue durée_ was not static geography — it was **historical geography** in the fullest sense: the analysis of how geographic conditions changed over centuries, and how those changes shaped human historical trajectories. His analysis of the **expansion of cereal cultivation** in the Mediterranean basin, the **deforestation** of Mediterranean mountains, the **advance and retreat of pastoralism**, and the **shifting geography of maritime trade routes** were all exercises in long-run historical geographic analysis that anticipated the contemporary fields of **historical ecology** and **environmental history**.

**The asymmetries of geographic evidence** — Braudel was acutely aware that geographic evidence was unevenly distributed in time and space: documentary sources were denser for cities than for rural areas, for literate elites than for illiterate peasants, for periods of commercial expansion than for periods of depression. His methodological response — the systematic use of geographic and archaeological evidence to complement and correct documentary sources — established a standard for the integration of multiple evidence types that continues to define best practice in historical geography.

**Critical assessment of Braudel**:

**Criticism 1 — The marginalisation of agency**: Braudel's tripartite temporal structure, despite its sophistication, tended toward **structural determinism**: by privileging _la longue durée_ — the geographic and climatic structures that changed most slowly — it implicitly minimised the significance of human agency, political choice, and historical contingency. As **Paul Ricoeur** argued in _**Temps et Récit**_ (_**Time and Narrative**_, 3 vols., 1983–1985), Braudel's model of historical time, while analytically powerful, had difficulty accommodating the **narrative dimension** of historical experience — the way in which individual and collective actors experienced their historical situation as meaningful, open, and subject to their choices.

**Criticism 2 — The unity of the Mediterranean**: Braudel's Mediterranean was a **constructed geographic unit** — not a natural region but an analytical choice. Critics including **Peregrine Horden** and **Nicholas Purcell** (_**The Corrupting Sea: A Study of Mediterranean History**_, 2000) have argued that the Mediterranean's apparent unity concealed enormous internal diversity — of climate, ecology, culture, political organisation, and economic development — that Braudel's macroscopic framework systematically obscured. The Mediterranean was not one sea but many.

**Criticism 3 — The underrepresentation of the non-Christian Mediterranean**: Braudel's Mediterranean was predominantly the Mediterranean of Christian Europe — the Ottoman Empire, the North African and Levantine coasts, and the Islamic world more broadly received systematically less attention than their historical and geographic significance merited. As **Houari Touati** argues in _**Islam et Voyage au Moyen Âge**_ (2000), the Islamic Mediterranean constituted a geographic world with its own specific spatial logics that Braudel's Eurocentric framework inadequately addressed.

## PART III — THE GERMAN MORPHOLOGICAL TRADITION

### 3.1 Otto Schlüter and the Cultural Landscape

**Otto Schlüter** (1872–1959) — geographer at the University of Halle (1906–1938) — was the theorist of the **cultural landscape** (_Kulturlandschaft_) concept that proved most productive in Phase III physical and cultural geography.

His foundational methodological statements — _"Bemerkungen zur Siedlungsgeographie"_ (_**Geographische Zeitschrift**_, 1899) and _"Die Ziele der Geographie des Menschen"_ (_**Münchner Geographische Studien**_, 1906) — argued for a **morphological** definition of geography: geography's proper object was the **landscape** — the visible, material configuration of Earth's surface as produced by natural processes and human activity. Geography was the discipline that studied the **forms** of the landscape — the shapes of terrain, the patterns of settlement, the network of roads, the mosaic of fields — as material expressions of the processes that had produced them.

Schlüter's distinction between:

_**Urlandschaft**_ (original landscape) — the natural landscape as it existed before significant human modification — the forest cover, the river pattern, the unmodified terrain of pre-agricultural Europe

_**Kulturlandschaft**_ (cultural landscape) — the landscape as transformed by human cultural activity: the cleared forests, the cultivated fields, the drained wetlands, the built settlements, the modified river courses that constituted the actual landscape observable in historical and contemporary Europe

The geographer's task was to understand the **transformation from _Urlandschaft_ to _Kulturlandschaft_**: how successive human cultures had progressively remade the natural landscape into a cultural one, leaving the material traces of their activity in the forms of the contemporary landscape.

**The methodological significance of Schlüter's framework**:

By defining geography's object as the **visible landscape** — what could be seen and mapped on the ground — Schlüter gave geography a clearly bounded and distinctively geographic subject matter. It was not the invisible social relations or mental processes studied by sociology and psychology; not the subsurface geological processes studied by geology; not the atmospheric dynamics studied by meteorology. It was the visible surface of Earth — the forms of terrain, vegetation, settlement, and land use — as they could be observed, described, and mapped by a geographer in the field.

This methodological conservatism — geography as the study of what was visible — had both strengths and limitations. Its strength was clarity: it gave geography a distinctive empirical domain that no other discipline claimed. Its limitation was that it excluded from geographic analysis precisely those invisible social forces — class relations, political power, cultural ideology — that most significantly determined which landscapes were produced and for whose benefit.

---

### 3.2 Alfred Hettner and the Chorology Principle

**Alfred Hettner** (1859–1941) — geographer at the University of Heidelberg (1899–1928); founder and editor of the _**Geographische Zeitschrift**_ (1895–1935) — was the most systematic theoretical geographer of Phase III in the German tradition and the primary influence on **Hartshorne's** anglophone reformulation.

Hettner's major methodological work — _**Die Geographie: Ihre Geschichte, ihr Wesen, und ihre Methoden**_ (_**Geography: Its History, Its Nature, and Its Methods**_, 1927) — argued for the **chorological principle** as geography's defining methodological commitment: geography studied the **character and differentiation of the Earth's surface** region by region — not through the accumulation of facts about individual phenomena but through the synthetic understanding of how different phenomena combined, in specific regions, to produce the distinctive geographic character of those regions.

His argument drew on **Kant's** classification of the sciences (as discussed in the Basics section) — geography as a **chorological science** organised by space, complementary to history as a **chronological science** organised by time, and both complementary to the **systematic sciences** (geology, meteorology, economics, sociology) organised by subject matter.

**Hettner's methodological programme** had three key components:

**The region as the integrating concept** — the region was not a mere convenience of organisation but the fundamental **geographic reality**: it was within regions that different phenomena — physical, biological, human — were combined in ways that produced geographic character. Geographic understanding required understanding regions as integrated wholes, not merely cataloguing their individual components.

**Chorology as methodology** — the chorological method required the geographer to identify the **specific combination** of physical and human factors that produced the character of each region, to trace the **relationships** between those factors, and to understand the **historical processes** through which the current geographic character had been produced.

**The geography-geology boundary** — Hettner argued that geography and geology were fundamentally different sciences despite their overlapping subject matter: geology studied the Earth's **historical development** through time, while geography studied the Earth's surface as it existed at a particular **cross-section in time**. This distinction — between the **diachronic** (historical, temporal) and the **synchronic** (geographic, spatial) — was Hartshorne's central methodological contribution to the anglophone tradition.

---

## PART IV — RICHARD HARTSHORNE AND THE PHILOSOPHICAL FOUNDATIONS OF REGIONAL GEOGRAPHY

### 4.1 _The Nature of Geography_ — The Definitive Methodological Statement

**Richard Hartshorne** (1899–1992) — geographer at the University of Wisconsin (1940–1970); President of the Association of American Geographers (1949) — produced in _**The Nature of Geography: A Critical Survey of Current Thought in the Light of the Past**_ (_**Annals of the Association of American Geographers**_, 29(3–4), 1939; published as separate volume, 1939) the most thorough and most philosophically sophisticated examination of geographic methodology produced in the anglophone tradition.

The work was a response to the perceived theoretical instability of American geography in the late 1930s: a discipline that had abandoned environmental determinism without a clear theoretical replacement, that was conducting regional and systematic research without a coherent justification for the relationship between them, and that was defining its boundaries with neighbouring disciplines in inconsistent and sometimes incoherent ways.

Hartshorne's project was simultaneously **historical** (a survey of geographic thought from the Greeks to the 1930s, focused on the German tradition), **philosophical** (an analysis of the epistemological foundations of geographic knowledge drawing on Kant, Hettner, and the philosophy of the natural sciences), and **prescriptive** (a definition of geography's proper scope and method that would provide a secure foundation for subsequent geographic practice).

---

### 4.2 Areal Differentiation — The Core Concept

Hartshorne's central theoretical claim — drawing directly on Hettner — was that geography's distinctive contribution to knowledge was **areal differentiation**: the systematic study of how and why the Earth's surface varied from area to area.

His formulation in _The Nature of Geography_:

> _"Geography is concerned to provide accurate, orderly, and rational description and interpretation of the variable character of the earth surface... that variation in character may be described most fully in terms of areal associations of phenomena related to each other and integrated in areas."_ — Hartshorne, _The Nature of Geography_, 1939, p. 462

Several elements of this definition require careful unpacking at the postgraduate level:

**"Variable character of the earth surface"** — geography's subject was not any particular class of phenomena (physical features, human populations, economic activities) but the **variation** of phenomena across Earth's surface. This was a **formal** rather than a **substantive** definition: geography was defined by the spatial dimension of its inquiry, not by what it studied.

**"Areal associations"** — the phenomena studied by geography were always studied in their **areal relationships**: not rocks as such (geology's domain) or populations as such (demography's domain) but rocks and populations and climates and land uses as they were **combined** in specific areas. This combination — the areal association of diverse phenomena — was geography's distinctive subject matter.

**"Integrated in areas"** — the phenomena of an area were not merely co-located but **integrated**: they mutually influenced each other to produce the distinctive character of the area as a whole. Understanding an area required understanding not just its component phenomena but the **relationships** between them.

---

### 4.3 The Idiographic-Nomothetic Distinction

Hartshorne's most philosophically significant — and most contested — argument was his defence of geography's **idiographic** character: its concern with the unique and particular rather than with the discovery of general laws.

Drawing on **Wilhelm Windelband's** (1848–1915) distinction between **nomothetic sciences** (those that sought general laws applicable to all instances of a class of phenomena) and **idiographic sciences** (those that sought to understand the unique, individual character of specific phenomena), Hartshorne argued that geography was fundamentally idiographic:

> _"The world of reality, as we find it, is unique; in consequence, the geography of that world must be unique. This is not a defect in geography as a science, but a consequence of the nature of its problem."_ — Hartshorne, _The Nature of Geography_, 1939, p. 448

This was a direct challenge to the assumption — shared by natural scientists and social scientists influenced by positivism — that scientific knowledge was necessarily nomothetic. Hartshorne argued that both idiographic and nomothetic sciences were scientifically legitimate; that geography's idiographic character reflected the nature of its problem (understanding specific areal combinations) rather than any scientific inadequacy; and that the demand that geography produce general spatial laws (which Schaefer would advance in 1953) was based on a misunderstanding of what scientific knowledge could and should be.

The idiographic defence was not a rejection of **generalisation** altogether — Hartshorne acknowledged that regional geography drew on the generalisations of systematic geography (geology, climatology, economics). But the **goal** of geographic synthesis was not to produce further generalisations — it was to understand the specific character of specific areas through the application of generalisations that were themselves the product of other disciplines.

**Schaefer's critique** (_"Exceptionalism in Geography"_, 1953) — which identified Hartshorne's position as **exceptionalism** (the unjustified claim that geography was exempt from the normal scientific requirement to seek general laws) — was, in one sense, correct: Hartshorne was arguing for a different conception of scientific knowledge than the positivist one. The question was whether Hartshorne's conception was philosophically defensible.

**The philosophical assessment**: Hartshorne's idiographic defence drew on a genuine philosophical tradition — the **hermeneutic** tradition, associated with **Wilhelm Dilthey** (1833–1911) and **Heinrich Rickert** (1863–1936), that distinguished the **natural sciences** (seeking general laws through the subsumption of particulars under universals) from the **cultural sciences** (seeking understanding of the individual and particular through empathetic interpretation). This distinction — which had been debated in German philosophy since the 1880s — was not simply an excuse for descriptive complacency but a serious philosophical argument about the different kinds of knowledge appropriate to different kinds of objects. **Ludwig von Wright's** _**Explanation and Understanding**_ (1971) provides the most rigorous subsequent treatment of this distinction.

---

### 4.4 _Perspective on the Nature of Geography_ (1959) — Revision and Defence

**Richard Hartshorne's** _**Perspective on the Nature of Geography**_ (1959) — published twenty years after _The Nature of Geography_ — was simultaneously a **defence** of the regional geography tradition against the quantitative revolution's emerging critique and an acknowledgment of the limitations that critique had identified.

Hartshorne conceded several points to the quantitative critics:

- Regional geography had **not been sufficiently attentive to theory**: the accumulation of regional descriptions without explicit theoretical frameworks was methodologically inadequate
- The relationship between **systematic** and **regional** geography needed to be rethought: systematic geography — the study of specific classes of phenomena across the whole world — should be understood not as an alternative to regional geography but as its **theoretical foundation**, providing the generalisations that regional synthesis applied to specific areas
- **Quantitative methods** were legitimate geographic tools and should be incorporated into geographic practice

But he maintained, against Schaefer, that the goal of geographic synthesis remained **areal differentiation** — understanding the unique character of specific portions of Earth's surface — and that this goal could not be reduced to the discovery of spatial laws without losing what was most distinctively geographic.

The 1959 _Perspective_ was, in retrospect, a rearguard action. The quantitative revolution had already taken hold in the most influential departments, and the theoretical arguments of spatial science — whatever their philosophical limitations — had the institutional momentum of novelty, methodological sophistication, and practical applicability that the regional tradition could not match.

---

## PART V — CARL SAUER AND THE BERKELEY SCHOOL

### 5.1 Sauer's Intellectual Position

**Carl Ortwin Sauer** (1889–1975) — Professor of Geography at the University of California, Berkeley (1923–1957); President of the Association of American Geographers (1940) — was the most original and most intellectually influential American geographer of Phase III. His Berkeley School dominated American cultural geography for a generation and established a tradition of cultural-historical geographic research that was simultaneously more empirically rigorous and more politically radical than the regional geography mainstream.

Sauer's intellectual formation was distinctive: trained at the University of Chicago under **Rollin Salisbury** (a geomorphologist in the Davisian tradition) and influenced by the German geographic tradition (particularly Schlüter) and the Chicago school of anthropology (particularly **Robert Redfield** and **Alfred Kroeber**), he developed a cultural geography that was simultaneously geomorphological (attentive to landform and environmental process), historical (committed to understanding landscapes as the products of long historical sequences), and anthropological (drawing on ethnographic and archaeological evidence).

---

### 5.2 _The Morphology of Landscape_ — The Manifesto

_"The Morphology of Landscape"_ (_**University of California Publications in Geography**_, 2(2), 1925, pp. 19–53) was Sauer's programmatic statement of cultural geography's scope and method — the most influential methodological paper in American Phase III geography.

Its famous formulation:

> _"The cultural landscape is fashioned from a natural landscape by a culture group. Culture is the agent, the natural area is the medium, the cultural landscape is the result. Under the influence of a given culture, itself changing through time, the landscape undergoes development, passing through phases, and probably reaching ultimately the end of its cycle of development. With the introduction of a different — that is, alien — culture, a rejuvenation of the cultural landscape sets in, or a new landscape is superimposed on remnants of an older one."_ — Sauer, "The Morphology of Landscape", 1925, p. 46

This formulation had several theoretical implications that distinguished it from both the Vidalian tradition and from environmental determinism:

**Culture as the active agent** — not the environment. The cultural landscape was produced by **cultural agency**, not environmental necessity. Different cultures inhabiting the same natural area would produce different cultural landscapes, depending on their technical capacity, their social organisation, and their cultural values and practices.

**The natural landscape as medium** — not determinant. The natural landscape provided the **material conditions** within which cultural agency operated — the soils, the vegetation, the landforms, the water resources — but it did not determine what cultural agency would do with those conditions. The same physical conditions supported radically different cultural landscapes under different cultural groups.

**The landscape palimpsest** — successive cultural occupations left successive layers of landscape transformation, producing a **palimpsest** — a landscape that bore the traces of multiple cultural groups at different historical moments. Understanding the contemporary cultural landscape required archaeological and historical investigation of its successive layers, not simply description of its current appearance.

**The cycle of cultural landscape development** — Sauer borrowed from Davis the concept of a developmental cycle, but transformed it: where Davis's cycle was driven by physical processes (erosion under geomorphological laws), Sauer's cycle was driven by **cultural processes** (the transformation of the natural landscape by a specific culture group). The cycle's stages and its endpoint were culturally rather than physically determined.

---

### 5.3 Sauer's Historical and Agricultural Geography

Sauer's most sustained and most original contributions were in **historical geography** — the study of how the cultural landscapes of the Americas had been produced through the long sequence of indigenous occupation, European colonisation, and post-colonial agricultural and environmental change.

_**Agricultural Origins and Dispersals**_ (1952) — his most influential monograph — argued for multiple independent origins of agriculture in different parts of the world, challenging the dominant assumption of a single origin in the Near East:

> _"The hearths of domestication were in the tropics, not in the subtropics; they were in areas of forest and woodland, not of grass; they were in areas of vegetative, not of seed, reproduction."_ — Sauer, _Agricultural Origins and Dispersals_, 1952, p. 24

Sauer proposed that the **earliest plant domestication** occurred not in the arid-zone Near Eastern hearth of **grain agriculture** (as **Vavilov's** conventional account suggested) but in the **humid tropical and subtropical zones** of Southeast Asia and the Americas, where **vegetative reproduction** (transplanting root crops) rather than seed planting was the primary agricultural technique. This hypothesis — contested at the time and still debated — was argued from a combination of biogeographic evidence (the distribution of cultivated plant varieties and their wild relatives), archaeological evidence (the distribution of early cultivation sites), and cultural-geographic reasoning (the characteristics of landscapes favourable to early plant exploitation).

_**The Early Spanish Main**_ (1966) — his final major monograph — was a historical geography of the Caribbean during the first decades of Spanish colonisation, showing how the specific geographic conditions of the islands (their ecological productivity, their indigenous populations, their resources) shaped the character of the earliest European colonial enterprise and its demographic and ecological consequences.

---

### 5.4 Sauer as Environmental Critic

**Carl Sauer's** commitment to environmental critique — the application of historical geographic analysis to the assessment of human-induced environmental change — made him one of the intellectual precursors of the **environmental movement** decades before environmentalism became a political force.

His address to the **Wenner-Gren Foundation Symposium** that produced _**Man's Role in Changing the Face of the Earth**_ (ed. **William L. Thomas**, 1956) — _"The Agency of Man on the Earth"_ — was a sustained indictment of modern industrial agriculture's destruction of the biological diversity and ecological stability that traditional agricultural systems had maintained:

> _"The modern spread of the machine and the shift to inanimate energy have gone far toward eliminating the variety of ways in which man has learned to live with the land. The current conversion of agriculture into an industry... is the most radical change in the ways of life of man that we have records of."_ — Sauer, "The Agency of Man on the Earth", in Thomas (ed.), _Man's Role in Changing the Face of the Earth_, 1956, p. 68

This critique — of **industrial monoculture**, of the loss of **agricultural biodiversity**, of the substitution of **fossil fuel energy** for biological energy in farming systems — anticipated the arguments of **Rachel Carson's** _**Silent Spring**_ (1962), **Wes Jackson's** _**Becoming Native to This Place**_ (1994), and the contemporary **agroecology** movement by decades.

---

### 5.5 The Berkeley School — Sauer's Students

Sauer trained a generation of cultural geographers who carried his approach through the mid-20th century and established the **historical-cultural geography** tradition as a permanent strand in American geographic thought:

**James Parsons** (1915–1997) — _**Antioqueño Colonization in Western Colombia**_ (1949); produced the definitive cultural-historical geography of Andean colonisation and land use transformation.

**Marvin Mikesell** (1929–2001) — comparative cultural landscape analysis in Morocco and North America; theoretical contributions to the cultural geography tradition.

**Philip Wagner** (1921–2000) — co-edited with **Marvin Mikesell** the definitive anthology of Sauerian cultural geography, _**Readings in Cultural Geography**_ (1962) — which defined the sub-field's scope for a generation.

**Donald Brand** — historical geography of Mexico and the American Southwest.

**George Carter** — historical biogeography and pre-Columbian cultural contacts.

The Berkeley School's limitations — its tendency toward **nostalgia** for pre-industrial cultures, its relative neglect of urban and industrial landscapes, its methodological conservatism — were identified by the next generation. **James Duncan's** _**The City as Text: The Politics of Landscape Interpretation in the Kandyan Kingdom**_ (1990) was the most theoretically sophisticated challenge from within the cultural geography tradition to the Sauerian heritage, arguing for a **poststructuralist retheorisation** of landscape that Sauer's morphological framework could not accommodate.

## PART VI — REGIONAL GEOGRAPHY IN BRITAIN AND NORTH AMERICA

### 6.1 The British Regional Tradition

British geography in Phase III was dominated by the regional tradition, strongly influenced by both the French Vidalian school and the German morphological tradition, and institutionally anchored in the **Royal Geographical Society** and the newly established university geography departments.

**Halford Mackinder** — whose geopolitical contributions were discussed in Phase II — also played a foundational role in establishing **geography as an educational subject** in British schools and universities. His address _"On the Scope and Methods of Geography"_ (_**Proceedings of the Royal Geographical Society**_, 1887) — delivered at the age of 25, one of the most influential methodological papers in British geographic history — argued for geography's educational value as the discipline that linked the natural sciences to the social sciences and provided the spatial framework for understanding the modern world.

**L. Dudley Stamp** (1898–1966) — the most practically influential British geographer of Phase III — directed the _**Land Utilisation Survey of Britain**_ (1931–1933): the most comprehensive geographic survey of land use ever conducted in Britain, mapping **every field** in England, Wales, and Scotland according to a systematic classification of land use types. The survey produced both the definitive empirical geography of Britain's agricultural land at a moment of severe agricultural depression and a **policy instrument** — Stamp's concept of **agricultural land classification** (Grades I–V, based on soil quality, drainage, and topographic conditions) — that directly informed post-war agricultural policy and land use planning.

As **Gordon Manley** argued in his obituary of Stamp (_Geographical Journal_, 1967), the Land Utilisation Survey was the most significant single exercise in **applied regional geography** in British history — demonstrating that systematic regional geographic analysis could provide the empirical foundation for evidence-based policy rather than simply producing academic descriptions.

**H.C. Darby** (1909–1992) — _**The Draining of the Fens**_ (1940); general editor of the _**A New Historical Geography of England**_ (1973) — established **historical geography** as a distinctive British sub-discipline, combining Vidalian attention to the long-run formation of cultural landscapes with British archival historical methods. His use of **Domesday Book** (1086) as a geographic source — mapping the agricultural and settlement geography of late 11th-century England from the Domesday evidence — was both a methodological innovation and a substantive achievement of the first order.

**The Institute of British Geographers** (IBG, founded 1933) — the professional association for British academic geographers — was established in part as a response to the RGS's domination by non-academic amateur geographers. Its foundation marked the **professionalisation** of British geography — the assertion of academic standards, peer-reviewed publication, and research-based disciplinary practice — that was a necessary precondition for the quantitative revolution that followed.

---

### 6.2 Isaiah Bowman and Applied Regional Geography in the United States

**Isaiah Bowman** (1878–1950) — Director of the American Geographical Society (1915–1935); geographic advisor to **Woodrow Wilson** at the Paris Peace Conference (1919); President of Johns Hopkins University (1935–1948) — was the most politically powerful American geographer of Phase III and the primary figure in American **applied regional geography**: the use of systematic regional geographic analysis in the service of state policy.

_**The New World: Problems in Political Geography**_ (1921) — his most widely read work — was a comprehensive regional geography of the post-war world, organised around the political-geographic problems created by the Treaty of Versailles: the new boundaries of Eastern Europe, the status of Germany's colonies, the mandated territories of the League of Nations, the competing claims in the Middle East. The work was simultaneously a regional geographic synthesis and a political-geographic argument — demonstrating how systematic geographic knowledge could inform the rational solution of territorial disputes.

**Neil Smith's** _**American Empire: Roosevelt's Geographer and the Prelude to Globalization**_ (2003) — the most thorough scholarly study of Bowman's career — demonstrates the extent to which Bowman's geographic work was inseparable from his political role: his boundary recommendations at Paris, his participation in **Franklin Roosevelt's** wartime policy planning (particularly the planning for post-war international economic institutions), and his role in the _M Project_ (the systematic study of potential sites for post-war resettlement of displaced European populations) were all exercises in the application of regional geographic knowledge to specific policy problems.

Bowman's career represents both the **highest achievement** of Phase III's applied ambitions — the demonstration that systematic regional geographic knowledge could inform significant policy decisions — and its deepest **political entanglement**: the inseparability of geographic analysis from the strategic interests of American imperial power. As **Smith** shows, Bowman's geography was not independent analysis that happened to be useful to power — it was analysis conducted from within power, shaped by its priorities, and directed toward its goals.

---

### 6.3 Preston James and the Regional Geography of Latin America

**Preston E. James** (1899–1986) — geographer at the University of Michigan (1923–1945) and Syracuse University (1945–1969) — produced in _**Latin America**_ (1942; 4th ed. 1969) the most comprehensive and most influential regional geography of a major world region produced in the anglophone tradition.

The work exemplified Phase III regional geography at its best: empirically comprehensive (drawing on extensive fieldwork across the continent), physically and humanly integrated (treating the physical geography of each region as the necessary context for understanding its human geography), historically attentive (tracing the pre-Columbian, colonial, and post-colonial geographic histories of each region), and analytically honest (acknowledging the complexity and diversity of a region that resisted easy generalisation).

James's concept of the _**habitat**_ — the specific geographic environment within which a human community lived and which its cultural practices had adapted to exploit — was a possibilist concept that drew on both the Vidalian _milieu_ and the Sauerian natural landscape to define the physical framework within which cultural-historical analysis operated.

His presidential address to the Association of American Geographers — _"Towards a Further Understanding of the Regional Concept"_ (_**Annals of the Association of American Geographers**_, 1952) — was one of the most honest assessments of Phase III regional geography's methodological difficulties: acknowledging that the region was an analytical construct rather than a natural given, that its boundaries were inevitably arbitrary to some degree, and that the coherence of regional character was always partial and contested rather than absolute.

---

## PART VII — THE QUANTITATIVE CHALLENGE AND THE DISSOLUTION OF PHASE III

### 7.1 Schaefer's Critique Revisited

**Fred K. Schaefer's** _"Exceptionalism in Geography: A Methodological Examination"_ (_**Annals of the Association of American Geographers**_, 43(3), 1953, pp. 226–249) — the text that most directly initiated the transition from Phase III to Phase IV — requires careful analysis at the postgraduate level, both for its intellectual content and for its institutional context.

**The intellectual argument**:

Schaefer argued that Hartshorne's defence of regional geography as the study of **unique areal combinations** was philosophically indefensible — an **exceptionalism** that exempted geography from the normal scientific requirement to seek general laws:

> _"Geometry, physics, chemistry, and even the biological sciences strive for morphological laws. Geography will have to do the same. The morphological laws at which geography aims are those that concern the spatial arrangements of phenomena on the surface of the earth."_ — Schaefer, "Exceptionalism in Geography", _AAAG_, 1953, p. 227

The argument drew on **logical positivism** — particularly the **covering law model** of scientific explanation associated with **Carl Hempel** (_**"Studies in the Logic of Explanation"**_, with Paul Oppenheim, _**Philosophy of Science**_, 1948) — to argue that genuine scientific explanation required the subsumption of particular cases under general laws. A geography that described unique areal combinations without deriving them from general spatial laws was not scientific — it was merely descriptive.

**The institutional context**:

**Hartshorne's** response — _"Exceptionalism in Geography Re-examined"_ (_**Annals of the Association of American Geographers**_, 1955) — identified several **contextual anomalies** in Schaefer's critique: Schaefer was not a trained geographer but an economist; his paper was written during a period of serious illness (he died of a heart attack shortly after it was accepted for publication); and some of its attribution of positions to Hartshorne appeared to misrepresent what Hartshorne had actually argued.

**David Harvey's** later assessment — in _**Explanation in Geography**_ (1969, pp. 49–53) — was more balanced: Schaefer's philosophical framework was in some respects poorly developed (he did not adequately distinguish between **morphological laws** and **process laws**), but his central challenge — that geography needed to seek general explanatory principles rather than confining itself to unique descriptions — was methodologically valid.

**The permanent significance of the Schaefer-Hartshorne debate**:

At the postgraduate level, the Schaefer-Hartshorne debate should be understood not as a debate with a winner and a loser but as the clearest statement of a tension — between the **idiographic** and the **nomothetic**, between the particular and the general, between description and explanation — that has been present in geographic thought since Eratosthenes and Strabo and that no subsequent paradigm has definitively resolved.

The quantitative revolution's spatial science demonstrated that geographic analysis could produce general spatial laws (or at least spatial regularities) of real predictive value. But it also demonstrated — through its failure to address the most geographically significant questions about place, power, and inequality — that general spatial laws without attention to the particular and the historical were insufficient for geographic understanding. The tension between Schaefer and Hartshorne is not resolved by either the quantitative revolution or by Phase V's critical geography — it is the disciplinary condition that both phases inhabit and that neither has transcended.

---

### 7.2 The Methodological Critique — The Region as Problem

By the early 1950s, the **methodological difficulties** of regional geography had been identified with sufficient clarity to constitute a genuine crisis of the paradigm. The regional geography tradition faced five interconnected methodological problems that it had never adequately resolved:

**The boundary problem** — how was the region to be defined and its boundaries determined? Different criteria (physical, cultural, economic, administrative) produced different regions with different boundaries. The _pays_ of Vidalian geography was too small for economic analysis; the world regions of general textbooks were too large for detailed study. No consensus had emerged on the appropriate scale of regional analysis or the appropriate criteria for regional definition.

**The uniqueness problem** — if every region was unique, what was the point of studying many regions? What knowledge transferred from the understanding of one region to the understanding of another? Regional geography accumulated particular knowledge without producing transferable understanding — it was idiographic in the most restricted sense.

**The theoretical vacuum** — regional geography had no explicit theory of why regions had the characters they had, or why they differed from each other. The _genre de vie_ concept explained how human communities adapted to their physical environments, but it did not explain why some communities were wealthy and others poor, why some regions were economically dynamic and others stagnant, why the spatial distribution of development was so systematically unequal.

**The descriptive formula** — the regional monograph had become formulaic: geological base, physical geography, historical settlement, contemporary land use, urban hierarchy. The formula produced a kind of knowledge — comprehensive, systematic, place-specific — but it did not produce **explanations**: it described what was without explaining why it was that way or how it had come to be that way.

**The urban problem** — Phase III regional geography had been developed primarily in the context of rural, agricultural landscapes. The _pays_, the _genre de vie_, the cultural landscape — these concepts were developed for and from the analysis of pre-industrial, agrarian geographic systems. By the mid-20th century, the world was rapidly urbanising. The conceptual tools of regional geography were poorly equipped to study cities, industrial regions, and the rapidly changing urban-industrial landscapes of post-war capitalism.

---

### 7.3 What the Regional Tradition Could Not Let Go

Despite its genuine methodological limitations, the regional tradition preserved several **intellectual commitments** that proved to be more important than the quantitative revolution's spatial science recognised:

**The irreducibility of the particular** — the insistence that places, regions, and landscapes had specific, historically produced characters that could not be fully captured by general spatial laws — that to understand a place you had to know it, not merely classify it. This insistence — which the quantitative revolution dismissed as pre-scientific description — was vindicated by Phase V's return to place as a central geographic concept and by the recognition that spatial science's universal laws were often uninformative about the specific geographic realities that most mattered.

**The physical-human integration** — at its best, regional geography maintained the Humboldtian commitment to integrating physical and human geographic analysis within a single synthetic account of specific places. The breakdown of this integration — increasingly sharp as physical geography aligned with natural sciences and human geography with social theory — was one of the most significant intellectual costs of the transition from Phase III to Phase IV.

**The synthetic ambition** — the aspiration to understand places in their full complexity — their geological structure, their climatic character, their ecological systems, their historical settlement, their economic organisation, their cultural practices — was intellectually ambitious in a way that the quantitative revolution's analytical focus on specific, measurable spatial regularities was not. The loss of this ambition produced a more rigorous but narrower geography.

**The writing tradition** — the regional monograph at its best — Vidal's _Tableau_, Braudel's _Mediterranean_, Sauer's _Agricultural Origins_, James's _Latin America_ — constituted a form of geographic writing that communicated the complexity and particularity of geographic reality to educated general audiences as well as to specialist scholars. The quantitative revolution's technical literature communicated within the discipline but not beyond it. The loss of the regional geographic writing tradition — the capacity to write about places in ways that were simultaneously rigorous and humane — was a cultural as well as an intellectual loss.

---

## PART VIII — THE ENDURING LEGACY OF PHASE III

### 8.1 The Return of the Region

Phase III's conceptual legacy was not simply superseded by subsequent paradigms — it was **transformed and recovered** in several important ways.

**New regionalism in economic geography** — the most significant revival of regional geographic thinking in Phase V — was explicitly indebted to the Marshallian-Vidalian tradition of understanding regional economic systems as the product of specific institutional configurations, historical trajectories, and relational networks.

**Allen J. Scott** (born 1938) — _**New Industrial Spaces**_ (1988); _**Metropolis: From the Division of Labor to Urban Form**_ (1988) — argued that the post-Fordist economic geography of the 1980s and 1990s was characterised by the emergence of new industrial districts based on **flexible specialisation** — geographically concentrated networks of small firms engaged in specialised complementary activities, held together by Marshallian external economies of knowledge spillovers, labour market pooling, and input-output linkages. Scott's industrial districts were recognisably Vidalian in their analytical logic: the specific character of each industrial district — its product specialisation, its firm network structure, its labour market characteristics — was the product of its specific historical and institutional trajectory, not derivable from general economic laws.

**Michael Storper** (born 1951) — _**The Regional World: Territorial Development in a Global Economy**_ (1997) — argued that regions persisted as the primary units of economic organisation in the global economy because they contained **untraded interdependencies** — technological knowledge, relational conventions, institutional frameworks, tacit skills — that could not be fully codified or transmitted across geographic distance. These untraded interdependencies were fundamentally Vidalian: they were the product of specific regional histories, specific relational configurations, specific cultural and institutional practices that had accumulated over time within specific geographic areas.

**Allen Scott** and **Michael Storper's** _"Regions, Globalization, Development"_ (_**Regional Studies**_, 2003) — one of the most cited papers in economic geography — argued explicitly for the region as the **primary scale** of economic development in the contemporary global economy: not the nation-state (whose economic significance had declined relative to both supranational and subnational levels), not the global (which was too abstract to be a site of economic organisation), but the **metropolitan region** — the city-region as the unit within which the knowledge economies of the 21st century were produced and reproduced.

This was new regionalism — theoretically sophisticated, quantitatively grounded, and economically rigorous — but it was also, in its deepest theoretical commitments, recognisably Phase III: the insistence on the geographic specificity of economic development, the importance of historically accumulated regional character, the irreducibility of regional difference to universal economic laws.

---

### 8.2 Place, Region, and Contemporary Geographic Theory

The return of **place** as a central concept in Phase V geography — through humanistic geography, feminist geography, and the progressive sense of place developed by **Massey** — was, at one level, a **reclamation** of what regional geography had preserved against the quantitative revolution's abstraction.

Tuan's topophilia, Relph's placelessness, Massey's progressive sense of place — these were theoretically more sophisticated than Vidal's _pays_ and Hartshorne's areal differentiation. But they were engaged with the same fundamental geographic question: what was the geographic significance of specific, particular places? How should geography understand the attachment of communities to specific portions of Earth's surface? What was the relationship between the local character of places and the global forces that shaped them?

Phase III had asked these questions without adequate theoretical resources to answer them. Phase V inherited both the questions and the inadequacy — and has produced more sophisticated theoretical frameworks (relational place, the power-geometry of mobility, place as meeting point of multiple trajectories) without fully resolving the tension between the particular and the general that Hartshorne and Schaefer debated in the 1950s.

---

### 8.3 Phase III and Contemporary Geographic Issues

**Regional development policy** — the use of geographic analysis to guide investment in lagging regions, to address the consequences of industrial decline, and to promote balanced territorial development — remains one of the most practically significant applications of geographic knowledge. The European Union's **Cohesion Policy** — the largest territorial development program in the world, allocating over €350 billion in Structural Funds and Cohesion Fund between 2021 and 2027 — is explicitly geographic in its logic: it defines **disadvantaged regions** using geographic indicators, targets investment to improve regional competitiveness, and evaluates outcomes using geographic measures of convergence and divergence.

The intellectual foundations of regional development policy are Phase III in origin — the insistence that regions matter as units of development, that regional economic performance cannot be explained by universal economic laws without attention to regional specificity, and that policy interventions must be tailored to the geographic character of specific regions rather than applied universally. **Phil Cooke's** _**Regions, Clusters and Creative Economies**_ (2012) and **Andrés Rodríguez-Pose's** _"The Revenge of the Places That Don't Matter (And What to Do About It)"_ (_**Cambridge Journal of Regions, Economy and Society**_, 2018) are the most important contemporary geographic contributions to regional development theory.

**Brexit and regional inequality** — **Rodríguez-Pose's** argument — that the Brexit vote, the election of Donald Trump, and the rise of populist nationalist politics across the advanced economies reflected the political mobilisation of communities left behind by economic globalisation — is a Phase III argument in contemporary dress: the insistence that geographic inequality between metropolitan regions and the periphery had geographic consequences that general economic models failed to predict and that regional geographic analysis could illuminate.

**The _longue durée_ and climate geography** — Braudel's concept of geographic time — the slow rhythms of climate and landscape change that underlie and condition historical processes — has found renewed relevance in **climate geography**. The analysis of how **paleoclimatic** conditions — the Medieval Warm Period, the Little Ice Age, the Holocene Climate Optimum — shaped the trajectories of historical civilisations is a recognisably Braudelian project, pursued by historical geographers including **Brian Fagan** (_**The Long Summer: How Climate Changed Civilization**_, 2004) and **Geoffrey Parker** (_**Global Crisis: War, Climate Change and Catastrophe in the Seventeenth Century**_, 2013).

_Phase III's contribution to geographic thought is not exhausted by its methodological limitations. Its insistence on the geographic significance of the particular, its commitment to physical-human synthesis, its synthetic writing tradition, and its demonstration that places have irreducible characters produced by specific historical trajectories — these are intellectual commitments that Phase IV's spatial science abandoned at significant cost and that Phase V's critical geography has partially recovered. The ongoing theoretical project of the discipline — the attempt to be simultaneously rigorous and place-sensitive, theoretically sophisticated and empirically grounded — is, in important ways, the attempt to produce what Phase III aspired to and could not quite achieve._
