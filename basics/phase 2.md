# PHASE II — THE AGE OF EXPLORATION AND ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINISM

## _17th Century to Early 20th Century_

### Postgraduate Lecture Notes — Research Level

---

## PREFATORY NOTE — METHODOLOGICAL ORIENTATION

Phase II is the most intellectually consequential and the most morally compromised phase in the history of geographic thought. It is the period in which geography achieved its greatest institutional power — its closest alignment with state authority, military strategy, and imperial administration — and simultaneously produced its most catastrophic theoretical framework: **environmental determinism**, the doctrine that the physical environment, and particularly climate, directly determined human character, cultural capacity, and civilisational achievement.

The postgraduate student of Phase II must resist two equally inadequate responses to this history.

The first is **exculpatory presentism**: the retrospective dismissal of environmental determinism as obviously wrong — the product of crude minds in a less enlightened era — which protects contemporary geography from engaging seriously with how a framework so empirically weak and ideologically loaded could have commanded the assent of the discipline's most distinguished practitioners for half a century. Environmental determinism was not the product of intellectual failure. It was the product of a **specific configuration of social interests, institutional incentives, theoretical assumptions, and political requirements** that gave it plausibility within its historical context — and understanding why requires sustained analysis, not moral condescension.

The second is **relativist historicism**: the suspension of critical judgment on the grounds that environmental determinists were simply "of their time" — that their ideas cannot be evaluated by contemporary standards. This is epistemologically and politically untenable. **Ellen Churchill Semple's** claim that tropical peoples were intellectually inferior to temperate-zone peoples was not merely wrong by contemporary standards — it was contestable on empirical grounds available at the time. The failure to contest it was itself a political choice, not an epistemological necessity.

**David N. Livingstone's** _**The Geographical Tradition**_ (1992) provides the most sophisticated framework for navigating between these inadequate responses — demonstrating that the history of geographic thought requires simultaneous attention to the **internal logic** of geographic ideas (what problems they were designed to solve, what evidence supported them, what internal coherence they possessed) and the **external social context** of their production (what interests they served, what institutional structures sustained them, what political consequences they generated).

This **contextual history of ideas** — neither purely internalist nor purely externalist — is the methodological standard required at the postgraduate level.

## PART I — THE CARTOGRAPHIC AND GEOGRAPHIC CRISIS OF 1492

### 1.1 The Discovery That Shattered a Paradigm

**October 12, 1492** — the landfall of **Christopher Columbus** on the island of **Guanahani** (identified by **Samuel Eliot Morison** in _**Admiral of the Ocean Sea**_, 1942, as Watling Island, now San Salvador, Bahamas) — was not merely a historical event. It was an **epistemological crisis**: the empirical demonstration that the geographic framework within which European scholarship had operated for over a millennium was fundamentally and irrecoverably incomplete.

The Greek _oikoumene_ — the inhabited world as Ptolemy had mapped it, covering approximately 180° of longitude from the Fortunate Isles to Southeast Asia — had no place for two entire continents housing tens of millions of people, an ocean larger than the Eurasian landmass, and ecological systems of complexity and richness that no classical author had imagined. As **J.H. Elliott** demonstrates in _**The Old World and the New, 1492–1650**_ (1970) — the foundational study of European intellectual responses to the discovery — the Americas were not simply an addition to existing geographic knowledge. They were a **negation** of the framework within which that knowledge had been organised.

The initial European response was **cognitive denial**: Columbus died (1506) maintaining that the lands he had reached were the eastern edge of Asia. **Amerigo Vespucci's** recognition — published in _**Mundus Novus**_ (1503) and _**Lettera**_ (1504) — that the lands constituted a _**Mundus Novus**_ (New World), unknown to the ancients, was the first published acknowledgment of the paradigmatic rupture. **Martin Waldseemüller's** _**Universalis Cosmographia**_ (1507) — the first map to label the new landmass **America** — gave the recognition cartographic form.

The Ptolemaic framework was simultaneously **validated and superseded**: its mathematical and projectional methods provided the tools for representing the expanded world, while its descriptive content — its _oikoumene_ — was shown to be catastrophically incomplete. The challenge facing Phase II geography was to reconstruct a geographic framework that could accommodate the new empirical reality while preserving the mathematical and scientific standards that Ptolemy had established.

---

### 1.2 The Cartographic Response — From _Oikoumene_ to Globe

The cartographic history of Phase II — from Waldseemüller's 1507 world map to Mercator's 1569 projection — constitutes the most rapid and consequential transformation in the history of cartography. Within 80 years of Columbus's landfall, European cartographers had produced world maps of adequate accuracy for oceanic navigation, established the mathematical foundations of modern cartographic practice, and institutionalised a set of representational conventions that shaped the geographic imagination of subsequent centuries.

**Martin Waldseemüller** (c. 1470–1520) — _**Universalis Cosmographia Secundum Ptholomaei Traditionem et Americi Vespucii Aliorum que Lustrationes**_ (1507) — the twelve-sheet world map produced at the _Gymnasium Vosagense_ in Saint-Dié-des-Vosges, of which only one copy survives (Library of Congress, Washington DC):

The map used **Ptolemy's second projection** (curved meridians, arced parallels) and Ptolemy's coordinate framework while extending it to encompass the newly discovered western landmass — labelled **America** for the first time, in honour of Vespucci. As **John Hessler** argues in _**The Naming of America**_ (2008), the map's most significant geographic claim was the depiction of America as a **separate continent** — not an appendage of Asia but a distinct landmass separated from it by a substantial ocean. This was a cartographic hypothesis that preceded empirical confirmation by six years: **Vasco Núñez de Balboa** crossed the Isthmus of Darien and sighted the Pacific only in 1513.

**Gerardus Mercator** (Gerhard Kremer, 1512–1594) — the most consequential cartographer of Phase II and arguably the most influential in the discipline's history. His two most important contributions:

_**Nova et Aucta Orbis Terrae Descriptio ad Usum Navigantium Emendate Accommodata**_ (1569) — the world map introducing the **Mercator projection**: a **cylindrical conformal projection** in which meridians are represented as parallel vertical lines (at constant spacing) and parallels as horizontal lines whose spacing increases with latitude to maintain conformality — the property that angles are preserved locally, meaning that a straight line on the map corresponds to a constant compass bearing (**loxodrome** or **rhumb line**) on the Earth's surface.

The navigational value of this property was enormous: a navigator could plot a constant compass course as a straight line on the chart, read off the required bearing, and maintain it throughout the voyage. For the oceanic navigation of the late 16th century — when compass bearings were the primary navigational instrument — this was transformative.

The political and representational consequences were equally significant. The Mercator projection **systematically inflates the apparent size of high-latitude landmasses** relative to equatorial ones — because the north-south spacing of parallels increases with latitude to maintain conformality, while the east-west spacing of meridians (constant in the projection) does not correspond to the actual east-west distances on the globe, which decrease with latitude. The result:

- Greenland appears approximately the same size as Africa — in reality Africa is approximately 14 times larger
- Europe appears larger than South America — in reality South America is approximately twice the size of Europe
- The Arctic regions appear as vast territories while the tropical regions appear compressed

As **Arno Peters** argued in his presentation of the **Peters projection** (1973) — an **equal-area cylindrical projection** that preserves relative area at the cost of shape distortion — the Mercator projection was not merely an aesthetic choice. It was a **political representation** that systematically enlarged the territories of the colonial powers (located at higher latitudes) relative to the colonised territories (located near the equator). The Mercator projection naturalised European geographic dominance by making European territories appear larger, and therefore more significant, than they actually were.

**The Peters-Mercator controversy** — documented by **John Loxton** (_"The Peters Phenomenon"_, _**Cartographic Journal**_, 1985) and assessed by **Mark Monmonier** in _**Rhumb Lines and Map Wars: A Social History of the Mercator Projection**_ (2004) — is one of the most instructive episodes in the history of cartographic politics. Peters' claim that the Mercator projection was an ideological instrument of imperialism was methodologically crude — the projection had been developed for navigational purposes, not to represent colonial power — but his broader point, that the choice of map projection was not politically neutral, was entirely correct. **J. Brian Harley's** _"Deconstructing the Map"_ (_**Cartographica**_, 1989) provided the theoretical framework for this insight: every cartographic choice — projection, scale, symbolisation, naming — is a political act, reflecting and reproducing specific social relations of power.

_**Atlas sive Cosmographicae Meditationes de Fabrica Mundi et Fabricati Figura**_ (1585–1595) — Mercator's three-volume world atlas, published posthumously in complete form — introduced the term _**atlas**_ to designate a bound collection of maps (named after the Titan Atlas who supported the heavens, not, as sometimes stated, after the African king Atlas). The atlas format — standardised maps of regions at comparable scales, assembled in a single volume organised by geographic region — became the dominant mode of geographic information organisation for the next three centuries and remains the standard format today.

### 1.3 The Epistemology of Exploration — Knowledge Production in the Age of Discovery

The Age of Exploration was not merely a period of geographic discovery — it was a **transformation in the social organisation of geographic knowledge production**. Understanding this transformation is essential for any critical engagement with Phase II geographic thought.

**The social organisation of exploration** — European maritime exploration was not the product of individual curiosity or philosophical inquiry. It was a **state-organised, capital-financed enterprise** directed toward specific economic and political objectives: the establishment of trade routes to Asia (bypassing the Ottoman-controlled land routes), the identification of resources exploitable for metropolitan accumulation, and the acquisition of territorial claims against rival European powers.

**The Portuguese** systematic programme of African coastal exploration — initiated under **Infante Henrique** (Henry the Navigator, 1394–1460) from the base at **Sagres** in the Algarve — was explicitly organised as a geographic intelligence operation: each successive expedition was required to return with standardised geographic information (charts, descriptions of coastlines, records of winds and currents, assessments of resources and peoples) that fed into a cumulative database maintained at the Portuguese court. As **Bailey Diffie** and **George Winius** document in _**Foundations of the Portuguese Empire, 1415–1580**_ (1977), this was the first **systematic state programme of geographic intelligence gathering** in European history — the prototype of the geographic intelligence operations that would characterise Phase II exploration.

**The politics of geographic naming** — the act of naming places was the primary instrument of colonial territorial claim. European explorers renamed places that already had names — overwriting the geographic vocabularies of indigenous peoples with European names that expressed possession (_**Nova Hispania**_, _**New England**_, _**Nieuw Amsterdam**_), commemoration (_**Cape of Good Hope**_, named by the Portuguese _**Cabo de Boa Esperança**_ in anticipation of the route to India), or description from a European perspective (_**Gold Coast**_, _**Ivory Coast**_, _**Slave Coast**_ — naming African regions by their utility to European extractors).

**J. Brian Harley** (_"New England Cartography and the Native Americans"_, in _**American Beginnings: Exploration, Culture, and Cartography in the Land of Norumbega**_, 1994) demonstrated that the cartographic erasure of indigenous place names was not incidental to European colonialism but constitutive of it: to replace a place name was to replace a geographic claim, to substitute one territorial imagination for another. **Keith Basso's** _**Wisdom Sits in Places: Landscape and Language Among the Western Apache**_ (1996) documents what was lost in this erasure: indigenous place names encoded detailed geographic knowledge of landscape, ecology, history, and social relationship — a geographic information system embedded in language.

**The knowledge hierarchies of exploration** — geographic knowledge produced through exploration was systematically filtered and selectively publicised. **Valentim Fernandes** and other early 16th-century compilers assembled geographic information from returning explorers; the Portuguese crown restricted access to navigational charts (_cartas de marear_) as state secrets; the **Casa de Contratación** (House of Trade) in Seville maintained the _**Padrón Real**_ (Royal Chart) — the official chart of the Spanish colonial world — as a classified document, updated from each returning expedition and consulted by licensed pilots. As **Alison Sandman** documents in _"Controlling Knowledge: Navigation, Cartography, and Secrecy in the Early Modern Spanish Atlantic"_ (in _**Science and Empire in the Atlantic World**_, 2007), the production and circulation of geographic knowledge in Phase II was governed by state power and commercial interest, not by the norms of open scientific communication.

## PART II — ALEXANDER VON HUMBOLDT AND THE FOUNDATION OF MODERN GEOGRAPHY

### 2.1 Humboldt's Place in the Discipline

**Alexander von Humboldt** (Friedrich Wilhelm Heinrich Alexander von Humboldt, 1769–1859) is, without serious intellectual competition, the most important figure in the history of modern geography — the scholar who transformed a discipline of description and administration into a **unified science of Earth** and established the theoretical principles that have guided the discipline's most productive moments ever since.

At the postgraduate level, Humboldt must be understood not merely as a historical figure but as a **living theoretical resource**: his concept of **Zusammenhang** (interconnectedness), his insistence on the integration of physical and human geographic analysis, his commitment to quantitative precision alongside aesthetic engagement, and his political geography of colonial critique are each more relevant to contemporary geographic challenges than most of what was produced between his death and the emergence of Phase V.

**Andrea Wulf's** _**The Invention of Nature: Alexander von Humboldt's New World**_ (2015) — the most widely read recent biography — provides an accessible introduction. **Malcolm Nicolson's** chapter _"Humboldtian Plant Geography after Humboldt: The Link to Ecology"_ (_**British Journal for the History of Science**_, 1987), **Michael Dettelbach's** _"Humboldtian Science"_ (in **N. Jardine, J.A. Secord** and **E.C. Spary**, eds., _**Cultures of Natural History**_, 1996), and **Anne Marie Claire Godlewska's** _**Geography Unbound: French Geographic Science from Cassini to Humboldt**_ (1999) provide the more rigorous scholarly assessments required at this level.

---

### 2.2 Biography and Intellectual Formation

Humboldt was born in Berlin in 1769 — the same year as Napoleon and Wellington — into a Prussian aristocratic family of moderate wealth. His intellectual formation was extraordinary in its range: he studied at the **University of Frankfurt an der Oder**, the **University of Göttingen** (where he encountered **Georg Forster** — naturalist on Cook's second voyage — whose influence was decisive), and the **Freiberg Mining Academy** (where his training in geology, mineralogy, and underground surveying gave him the quantitative and empirical skills that distinguished his subsequent fieldwork from the armchair synthesis of most contemporary natural historians).

His relationship with **Johann Wolfgang von Goethe** — whom he met in 1794 and maintained as a close intellectual interlocutor for decades — was philosophically formative: Goethe's **Naturphilosophie** — the conviction that nature was an organic whole whose parts were intelligible only in relation to each other — reinforced and enriched Humboldt's developing concept of geographic interconnectedness. As **Robert J. Richards** demonstrates in _**The Romantic Conception of Life: Science and Philosophy in the Age of Goethe**_ (2002), the relationship between Romantic philosophy of nature and Humboldtian science was not one of opposition (science vs. Romanticism) but of creative synthesis.

His **five-year expedition to Spanish America** (1799–1804) — undertaken with the French botanist **Aimé Jacques Alexandre Bonpland** (1773–1858), authorised by the Spanish Crown through the extraordinary patronage of **Mariano Luis de Urquijo** (Minister of State under Charles IV), and covering approximately 60,000 kilometres through Venezuela, Cuba, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and Mexico — was the scientific enterprise upon which his entire subsequent theoretical framework was grounded.

The expedition's **empirical scope** was unprecedented: Humboldt and Bonpland collected approximately 60,000 plant specimens (representing approximately 6,300 species, of which approximately 2,000 were new to science); made continuous meteorological measurements using standardised instruments (barometer, thermometer, hygrometer, electrometer, cyanometer — the latter invented by Humboldt to measure the blueness of the sky); measured the **magnetic inclination and intensity** at hundreds of stations across the continent; conducted astronomical observations to determine latitudes and longitudes; measured ocean temperatures (identifying what is now called the **Humboldt Current** — the cold coastal upwelling current off Peru, though Humboldt himself was careful to note that he had not discovered it); and ascended **Chimborazo** (then believed to be the world's highest mountain) to an altitude of approximately 5,878 metres — higher than any European had previously climbed.

---

### 2.3 The Concept of Zusammenhang — Interconnectedness as Geographic Principle

Humboldt's foundational theoretical contribution — the concept that distinguished his geography from all previous traditions — was his insistence that the proper subject of geographic inquiry was not the **distribution** of phenomena but their **interconnection**: the relationships between climate, vegetation, geology, landform, ocean current, atmospheric composition, and human activity that produced the distinctive character of each portion of Earth's surface.

He articulated this vision most completely in _**Cosmos: A Sketch of a Physical Description of the Universe**_ (_**Kosmos: Entwurf einer physischen Weltbeschreibung**_, 5 vols., 1845–1862 — the last volume published posthumously):

> _"In considering the study of physical phenomena, not merely in its bearings on the material wants of life, but in its general influence on the intellectual advancement of mankind, we find its noblest and most important result to be a knowledge of the chain of connection, by which all natural forces are linked together, and made mutually dependent upon each other."_ — Humboldt, _Cosmos_, vol. I (Otte translation, 1849), p. 23

This passage contains the essential Humboldtian geographic epistemology. The goal of geographic inquiry was not to accumulate facts about individual phenomena — to count species, measure temperatures, record altitudes — but to understand the _**chain of connection**_ that linked these phenomena into an integrated natural system. Facts were the raw material; connection was the scientific objective.

This was not merely a methodological preference. It was a **philosophical commitment** with profound implications for the relationship between physical and human geography. For Humboldt, the climate of a region, its vegetation, its soils, its river systems, its geological structure, and the character of its human inhabitants were not separate objects of parallel description but **mutually determining elements of a single geographic whole**. You could not understand any one of them without understanding all of them — and their relationships to each other.

**The Naturgemälde** (_**Geographie der Pflanzen in den Tropenländern: ein Naturgemälde der Anden**_, 1807 — the plant geography of the Andes, produced with Bonpland and the French botanist **Karl Sigismund Kunth**) — was the visual expression of this theoretical vision: a detailed cross-sectional profile of Chimborazo showing, at each altitude zone, the characteristic vegetation, the associated fauna, the temperature and barometric pressure, the snowline, the limit of perpetual snow, and human land uses. This was not a distribution map — it was a **relational diagram**: a representation of how all these phenomena varied together with altitude, constituting the integrated geographic character of the mountain.

As **Nicolson** (1987) demonstrates, the Naturgemälde established the concept of the **altitudinal vegetation zone** — the systematic change in vegetation type with altitude that replicated, in compressed vertical space, the latitudinal change from equatorial forest to polar tundra — and thereby demonstrated that geographic patterns were not random but the expression of underlying physical laws governing the relationship between climate (primarily temperature) and biological community.

---

### 2.4 Humboldtian Science — Methodology and Practice

**Michael Dettelbach** (1996) coined the term **Humboldtian science** to designate the specific scientific practice that Humboldt developed and that distinguished his approach from both the Linnaean natural history tradition (which classified phenomena into taxonomic categories) and the Newtonian mathematical physics tradition (which sought universal quantitative laws).

Humboldtian science was characterised by:

**Instrumental precision** — the insistence on standardised, carefully calibrated instruments for all measurements, enabling comparison across different locations and times. Humboldt's obsessive attention to instrument calibration — checking his barometers against known standards, correcting his thermometers for instrument error, establishing atmospheric pressure baselines at sea level — established **quantitative comparability** as a norm of geographic fieldwork.

**Global simultaneity** — the aspiration to measure the same phenomena simultaneously at different locations around the world, enabling the identification of spatial patterns that local measurement could not reveal. Humboldt was the primary organiser of the **Magnetic Crusade** of the 1830s — the global network of standardised magnetic observatories established at his instigation that produced the data for **Carl Friedrich Gauss's** mathematical theory of Earth's magnetic field. This was the first **global geographic data network** — the direct ancestor of the World Meteorological Organization's global observing system.

**Isoline mapping** — Humboldt introduced the **isoline** (a line connecting points of equal value) as a cartographic tool for representing continuous spatial distributions. His 1817 map of mean annual temperatures — using what he called _**isothermal lines**_ (later standardised as **isotherms**) — was the first isoline map in geographic history and established the representational convention that underlies all subsequent meteorological, climatological, oceanographic, and topographic mapping. As **Arthur Robinson** documents in _**Early Thematic Mapping in the History of Cartography**_ (1982), Humboldt's introduction of the isoline was the most important innovation in thematic cartography between Ptolemy and GIS.

**Transdisciplinary synthesis** — the explicit crossing of disciplinary boundaries to understand geographic phenomena in their full complexity. Humboldt moved between geology, botany, zoology, climatology, oceanography, anthropology, political economy, and aesthetics not because he lacked disciplinary rigour but because geographic reality required all of these perspectives simultaneously. His insistence that specialisation without synthesis was scientific inadequacy is a permanent challenge to the increasing disciplinary fragmentation of geographic knowledge.

---

### 2.5 The Essay on the Geography of Plants

_**Essai sur la Géographie des Plantes**_ (_**Essay on the Geography of Plants**_, 1807) — the first major publication from the American expedition, co-authored with Bonpland — constituted the founding document of **plant geography** (_**Pflanzengeographie**_) as a scientific discipline.

Its central argument: the distribution of plant species and vegetation communities across Earth's surface was not random but was determined by **systematic physical geographic factors** — primarily temperature, moisture, light, and soil — operating at multiple geographic scales simultaneously. Understanding plant distributions therefore required:

1. **Global-scale analysis** — the identification of major vegetation zones corresponding to broad climatic belts (the precursor of the modern **biome** concept)
2. **Regional-scale analysis** — the identification of regional vegetation patterns corresponding to local climatic and geological conditions
3. **Local-scale analysis** — the identification of site-specific vegetation communities corresponding to microclimate, soil chemistry, and topographic position

This **multi-scalar** framework — the explicit recognition that geographic patterns operated simultaneously at different scales and required analysis at each scale — was one of Humboldt's most important methodological contributions. It anticipated the **scalar relativism** of contemporary geographic theory by nearly two centuries.

The _Essay_ also contained one of the earliest systematic discussions of **human alteration of natural vegetation** — the recognition that the vegetation patterns Humboldt observed in the Americas were not purely natural but had been modified by indigenous burning, cultivation, and land management practices over millennia. This was the seed of what **William L. Thomas** (ed.) would develop into _**Man's Role in Changing the Face of the Earth**_ (1956) — the foundational text of environmental geography — at the symposium that Humboldt's intellectual tradition directly inspired.

---

### 2.6 Humboldt's Political Geography — Against Colonialism

The aspect of Humboldt's work most consistently underemphasised in anglophone accounts — and most relevant to contemporary postcolonial geography — was his **systematic political-economic critique of European colonialism**.

His _**Political Essay on the Kingdom of New Spain**_ (_**Essai Politique sur le Royaume de la Nouvelle-Espagne**_, 2 vols., 1808–1811) — the most detailed geographic and economic analysis of any colonial territory produced in the early 19th century — documented the systematic economic distortions of Spanish colonial administration: the concentration of land in the hands of a colonial elite, the forced labour of indigenous peoples in mines and haciendas, the destruction of pre-Columbian agricultural systems, the suppression of manufacturing to maintain colonial dependency on metropolitan imports, and the extraction of silver and other primary commodities at the expense of local economic development.

Humboldt's analytical framework anticipated, by several decades, what **André Gunder Frank** would theorise in _**The Development of Underdevelopment**_ (1966) as **dependency theory**: the argument that the poverty of colonial territories was not the result of their inherent backwardness but of the systematic extraction of wealth by metropolitan powers and the structural distortion of colonial economies to serve metropolitan interests.

His _**Political Essay on the Island of Cuba**_ (_**Essai Politique sur l'Île de Cuba**_, 1826) was the most sustained geographic analysis of the **slave economy** produced in the early 19th century. Humboldt's conclusion:

> _"If mines are to be considered as sources of wealth, the labor of slaves, under the burning skies of the tropics, offers the most revolting spectacle that human industry can present. With what name shall we designate that state of society in which man, without any solitary enjoyment, without any hope, sacrifices his existence to augment, by forced labor, the production of sugar, coffee and cotton?"_ — Humboldt, _Political Essay on the Island of Cuba_ (Montgomery translation, 1856), p. 217

This passage — combining geographic analysis (the tropical plantation economy) with moral condemnation (the slave system) — exemplifies what **Vera Candiani** (_"Humboldt's Political Geography of the New World"_, in _**New World Orders: Violence, Sanction, and Authority in the Colonial Americas**_, 2005) identifies as Humboldt's distinctive contribution to geographic ethics: the use of geographic analysis as an instrument of political critique.

The American publisher of the English translation, **John S. Thrasher**, omitted the anti-slavery sections from the 1856 American edition — demonstrating that geographic texts, even in translation, were subject to political censorship. As **Aaron Sachs** argues in _**The Humboldt Current: Nineteenth-Century Exploration and the Roots of American Environmentalism**_ (2006), Humboldt's political geography was systematically suppressed in the American geographic tradition precisely because it challenged the ideological foundations of American slavery and territorial expansion.

---

### 2.7 Humboldt's Legacy — Intellectual Genealogy

Humboldt's influence on subsequent geographic thought was **direct, extensive, and transformative** — though not always acknowledged:

**Charles Darwin** (1809–1882) — explicitly identified Humboldt's _**Personal Narrative**_ as the primary inspiration for his voyage on the _Beagle_: _"My whole course of life is due to having read and re-read as a youth his Personal Narrative"_ (letter to J.D. Hooker, 1845, in _**The Life and Letters of Charles Darwin**_, ed. Francis Darwin, 1887, vol. I, p. 336). The ecological and biogeographic dimensions of _**On the Origin of Species**_ (1859) — the attention to geographic distribution as evidence for evolution, the analysis of island biogeography, the concept of ecological competition — are directly traceable to Humboldtian geography.

**Alfred Russel Wallace** (1823–1913) — co-discoverer of natural selection, founder of biogeography as a systematic discipline (_**The Geographical Distribution of Animals**_, 2 vols., 1876; _**Island Life**_, 1880) — was equally indebted to Humboldt. Wallace's biogeographic regionalism — the identification of major biogeographic realms defined by their distinctive fauna, separated by barriers to dispersal (the **Wallace Line** between the Oriental and Australian realms) — was a direct extension of Humboldtian plant geography to the animal kingdom.

**George Perkins Marsh** (1801–1882) — _**Man and Nature, or Physical Geography as Modified by Human Action**_ (1864) — applied Humboldtian principles of interconnection to the analysis of human environmental transformation, arguing that European deforestation, soil erosion, and river regulation were disrupting the natural systems that Humboldt had described, with potentially catastrophic long-term consequences. Marsh's work is the founding text of **environmentalism** and **conservation geography** — and it is explicitly Humboldtian in its intellectual framework.

**August Grisebach** (1814–1879) — _**Vegetation der Erde**_ (1872) — systematised Humboldt's vegetation geography into a comprehensive classification of **vegetation formations** (the precursor of the modern **biome**), providing the taxonomic framework that **A.F.W. Schimper** would develop in _**Pflanzengeographie auf physiologischer Grundlage**_ (_**Plant Geography upon a Physiological Basis**_, 1898) into the standard pre-ecological account of global vegetation distribution.

**A.G. Tansley** (1871–1955) — who coined the term **ecosystem** in _"The Use and Abuse of Vegetational Concepts and Terms"_ (_**Ecology**_, 1935) — was working within a tradition of ecological thinking that was directly descended from Humboldtian geography through the plant sociology of **Frederic Clements** and **Eugenius Warming**.

## PART III — CARL RITTER AND SYSTEMATIC GEOGRAPHY

### 3.1 Ritter's Project

**Carl Ritter** (1779–1859) — who died, with extraordinary symbolic appropriateness, in the same year as Humboldt — was Humboldt's great contemporary and the co-founder (with Humboldt) of modern academic geography. His relationship to Humboldt was not one of simple agreement but of **productive divergence**: where Humboldt approached geography through fieldwork and natural science, Ritter approached it through textual synthesis and philosophical idealism; where Humboldt emphasised natural systems, Ritter emphasised the **relationship between physical geography and human history**.

Ritter held the first **professorship of geography** at a German university — established at the University of Berlin in 1820, where he taught until his death. His multi-volume _**Erdkunde im Verhältniß zur Natur und zur Geschichte des Menschen**_ (_**Earth Science in Relation to Nature and the History of Mankind**_, 19 vols., 1817–1859) — the most ambitious systematic comparative geography ever attempted — was left incomplete at his death, covering only Asia and Africa.

---

### 3.2 Ritter's Theoretical Framework — Teleological Geography

Ritter's geographical framework was explicitly **teleological** — grounded in a **Protestant theological** conviction that the physical configuration of Earth was divinely designed to produce specific historical outcomes. This was not environmental determinism in the secular sense of Semple or Huntington — it was a **providential geography**: the belief that God had shaped continents, coastlines, river systems, and mountain ranges to serve as the geographic preconditions for the providentially ordered development of human civilisation.

As Ritter stated in his programmatic essay _"Über das historische Element in der geographischen Wissenschaft"_ (_**On the Historical Element in Geographic Science**_, 1833):

> _"The earth and its inhabitants stand in the closest reciprocal relations, and one cannot be truly presented without the other. History, therefore, and geography must be studied together. Land influences inhabitants, and inhabitants influence the land; the physical and historical world are indissolubly united."_ — Ritter, cited in **Hanno Beck**, _Carl Ritter: Genius der Geographie_ (1979), p. 147 (author's translation)

The teleological framework produced several specific geographic arguments:

**Continental shape and historical development** — Ritter argued that the shape of continents was not geographically accidental but purposively configured. Europe's **deeply indented coastline** — its numerous peninsulas, gulfs, and islands — maximised the ratio of coastline to area, providing natural harbours, maritime accessibility, and the geographic conditions for the commercial, maritime civilisation that had produced European historical dynamism. Africa's **smooth, regular coastline** — with few natural harbours and rivers interrupted by falls and cataracts — explained (for Ritter) Africa's relative isolation and the supposed retardation of African historical development.

**The Mediterranean as geographic cradle** — Ritter's analysis of the Mediterranean basin as the geographic matrix within which European and Near Eastern civilisation had developed — sheltered from the open ocean, connected by navigable waters, surrounded by diverse but interacting climatic and ecological zones — anticipates **Fernand Braudel's** _**The Mediterranean**_ (1949) by over a century, though the providential framework that sustains Ritter's analysis was entirely foreign to Braudel's historical materialism.

**River systems and civilisational geography** — Ritter argued that the great river systems of the world — the Nile, the Tigris-Euphrates, the Indus, the Ganges, the Yellow River — were not merely physical features but the geographic preconditions for the riverine civilisations that had first developed complex political organisation, writing, and urban life. This was not determinism in the strong sense — it was a possibilist argument that the river systems _enabled_ civilisational development without _necessitating_ it.

---

### 3.3 Ritter's Influence and Legacy

Ritter's influence was exercised primarily through his institutional position — his Berlin professorship made him the teacher of a generation of German geographers — and through the **organisational framework** of the _Erdkunde_, which established the systematic regional geography of each continent as the primary unit of geographic synthesis.

His most direct intellectual heir in the anglophone world was **Arnold Henry Guyot** (1807–1884) — Swiss-born geographer who studied under Ritter in Berlin, emigrated to the United States in 1848, and held the first geography professorship at Princeton (1854). Guyot's _**The Earth and Man: Lectures on Comparative Physical Geography in its Relation to the History of Mankind**_ (1849) — explicitly Ritterian in framework — was widely used as a geography textbook in American schools and universities in the second half of the 19th century, transmitting a **teleological, providentialist geography** to American geographic education at the moment when environmental determinism was about to assert itself.

The **critical significance of Ritter** for Phase II is his role as the **theoretical hinge** between Humboldtian natural science and environmental determinism. Ritter's framework was more sophisticated than Semple's or Huntington's — it was teleological rather than mechanistic, possibilist in structure if not always in practice — but its argument that physical geography purposively shaped human historical development provided the intellectual scaffolding that environmental determinists would later inhabit with a secular and mechanistic content that Ritter himself would have rejected.

## PART IV — ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINISM

### 4.1 The Doctrine and Its Genealogy

**Environmental determinism** — the doctrine that the **physical environment**, particularly **climate**, directly determines human character, cultural achievement, and civilisational capacity — was the dominant theoretical framework of academic geography from approximately 1880 to 1930 and the most consequential geographic idea of Phase II.

Its genealogy was ancient — traceable, as demonstrated in Phase I, to **Parmenides'** five-zone model and **Aristotle's** climate-character argument in the _Politics_. Its more immediate intellectual predecessors included:

**Jean Bodin** (1530–1596) — _**Les Six Livres de la République**_ (1576) — the most sophisticated Renaissance statement of the climate-character argument: peoples of the north were warlike but intellectually limited; peoples of the south were imaginative but servile; peoples of the temperate middle were best fitted for self-governance.

**Charles-Louis de Secondat, Baron de Montesquieu** (1689–1755) — _**De l'esprit des lois**_ (_**The Spirit of the Laws**_, 1748) — the Enlightenment's most influential statement of geographic determinism. Montesquieu argued that climate directly shaped human character (cold climates produced vigour, courage, and the love of liberty; hot climates produced languor, timidity, and submissiveness) and that political institutions should be adapted to climate rather than imposed universally:

> _"Cold air constricts the extremities of the external fibres of the body; this increases their elasticity, and favours the return of the blood from the extreme parts to the heart. It contracts those muscles whose extremities are skin; it therefore adds to their force. Hot air, on the contrary, relaxes and lengthens the extremes of the fibres; it therefore diminishes their force and elasticity."_ — Montesquieu, _The Spirit of the Laws_, Book XIV, Chapter 2 (Nugent translation, 1748)

This physiological mechanism — the idea that climate acted on human character through its effects on the nervous fibres of the body — gave Montesquieu's determinism a quasi-scientific basis that distinguished it from purely philosophical climate theory. It was speculative physiology — **wrong** — but it was stated in the language of natural science rather than philosophical authority.

**Henry Thomas Buckle** (1821–1862) — _**History of Civilization in England**_ (2 vols., 1857–1861) — the Victorian historian who most directly transmitted deterministic climate theory to the late 19th century academic tradition, arguing that the history of civilisation was fundamentally determined by geographic factors — climate, soil, food, and landscape — acting on human intelligence and character.

---

### 4.2 Friedrich Ratzel — The Scientific Foundation

**Friedrich Ratzel** (1844–1904) — geographer, journalist, and political scientist; Professor of Geography at the University of Munich (1876–1886) and Leipzig (1886–1904) — was the most important and the most intellectually complex figure of the environmental determinist tradition. His work was simultaneously more sophisticated and more dangerous than that of his successors.

**Biographical and intellectual formation** — Ratzel trained as a zoologist under **Ernst Haeckel** — the Darwinian biologist who coined the term **ecology** (_Ökologie_, 1866) — and the biological framework of his geographic thought was deep rather than superficial. He was not simply importing biological metaphors into geography; he was attempting to apply what he understood as genuinely Darwinian principles — natural selection, the struggle for existence, adaptation to environment — to the geographic analysis of human societies.

_**Anthropogeographie**_ (2 vols., 1882–1891):

Volume I — _**Grundzüge der Anwendung der Erdkunde auf die Geschichte**_ (_**Foundations of the Application of Earth Science to History**_, 1882) — argued that human history was fundamentally shaped by geographic factors, especially the physical environment. This was not simple determinism — Ratzel acknowledged the role of cultural transmission and historical accident — but the **long-run tendency** of geographic influence was, in his framework, decisive.

Volume II — _**Die geographische Verbreitung des Menschen**_ (_**The Geographical Distribution of Mankind**_, 1891) — applied geographic analysis to the distribution of human cultures and technologies across Earth's surface, anticipating (in methodological approach if not in theoretical framework) the cultural-historical biogeography of **Carl Sauer**.

_**Politische Geographie**_ (_**Political Geography**_, 1897) — established the systematic study of the geographic dimensions of political power: the relationship between territory, resources, population, and state capacity. Ratzel's foundational concepts:

**The organic state** — Ratzel's most influential and most dangerous idea: the **analogy between states and organisms**. Like organisms, states required space (territory) to sustain themselves; like organisms, they competed with neighbours for resources; like organisms, they grew, declined, and died. This was an **analogy**, not an identity — Ratzel was sufficiently sophisticated to know that states were not literally organisms — but the logic of the analogy was clear and its political implications were explosive.

_**Lebensraum**_ (living space) — the concept that politically and culturally vigorous peoples required sufficient territorial space to sustain their vitality. A people confined within inadequate territory would experience demographic and cultural decline, like an organism in an undersized environment; expansion into new territory was therefore not aggression but a natural geographic necessity, analogous to the growth of an organism.

As **Woodruff D. Smith** demonstrates in _**The Ideological Origins of Nazi Imperialism**_ (1986) — the definitive study of the intellectual genealogy — _**Lebensraum**_ in Ratzel's original formulation was a **descriptive geographic concept**: an attempt to apply ecological thinking to the analysis of political geography. It was not a prescription for conquest. But the logical structure of the concept — territorial expansion as natural necessity, the struggle between peoples for geographic space as the geographic equivalent of natural selection — was available for ideological appropriation. **Karl Haushofer** (1869–1946) and the **Munich School of Geopolitics** (_Geopolitik_) developed Ratzlian concepts into the theoretical framework that influenced **Rudolf Hess**, was absorbed into Nazi strategic thinking, and provided partial intellectual legitimation for the territorial expansionism that produced the Second World War.

The question of **intellectual responsibility** — how far Ratzel bears responsibility for the use made of his concepts by subsequent ideologues — is one of the most important questions in the history of geographic thought. **Gearóid Ó Tuathail's** _**Critical Geopolitics**_ (1996) and **Günter Buttmann's** _**Friedrich Ratzel: Leben und Werk eines deutschen Geographen**_ (1977) approach this question from different perspectives. The most defensible position is that Ratzel bears **partial but not unlimited responsibility**: the logical structure of _**Lebensraum**_ made it available for appropriation in ways that Ratzel's more nuanced formulations did not prevent; but the specific political uses to which it was put required additional ideological ingredients — racial theory, Nazi political theology, historical contingency — that were not present in Ratzel's own work.

---

### 4.3 Ellen Churchill Semple — American Environmental Determinism

**Ellen Churchill Semple** (1863–1932) — the first woman elected President of the Association of American Geographers (1921); Professor at Clark University (1921–1932) — was the primary figure in transmitting Ratzlian environmental determinism to the anglophone world. She studied under Ratzel in Leipzig (1891–1892) — at a time when German universities did not formally admit women, requiring special arrangements — and spent her subsequent career translating his framework into English and applying it to American and Mediterranean geographic problems.

_**Influences of Geographic Environment: On the Basis of Ratzel's System of Anthropo-Geography**_ (1911) — her major work — was the most widely read academic geography text in the United States for at least a generation and the clearest statement of environmental determinism in English. The opening passage — already cited in the Basics section — set the tone:

> _"Man is a product of the earth's surface. This means not merely that he is a child of the earth, dust of her dust; but that the earth has mothered him, fed him, set him tasks, directed his thoughts, confronted him with difficulties that have strengthened his body and sharpened his wits, given him his problems of navigation or irrigation, and at the same time whispered hints for their solution. She has entered into his bone and tissue, into his mind and soul."_ — Semple, _Influences of Geographic Environment_, 1911, p. 1

The rhetorical strategy of this passage is significant at the postgraduate level: the naturalising metaphor of **motherhood** (the earth as mother) presented a deterministic relationship as nurturing and benign rather than constraining and hierarchical. Semple's determinism was not explicitly racial in the crude sense of Huntington — she focused on physical environment rather than racial stock — but its political implications were identical: the geographic distribution of cultural and civilisational achievement was natural, not political; the dominance of temperate-zone peoples over tropical peoples was a geographic fact, not a political arrangement.

_**The Geography of the Mediterranean Region: Its Relation to Ancient History**_ (1931) — her second major work, published the year before her death — applied the environmental determinism framework to Mediterranean historical geography. Despite its deterministic theoretical apparatus, it contains detailed and often insightful regional geographic analysis that retains some scholarly value.

**Critical assessment of Semple** — **Cynthia Stokes Brown** (_"Reflections on Ellen Churchill Semple"_, _**Geographical Review**_, 1982) and **Mona Domosh** and **Joni Seager** (_**Putting Women in Place: Feminist Geographers Make Sense of the World**_, 2001) provide the most nuanced assessments. Semple's career was simultaneously a professional achievement — breaking significant gender barriers in a male-dominated discipline — and an intellectual failure — building that career on a theoretical framework that was empirically weak and politically complicit. The two dimensions cannot be separated: her professional credibility within an androcentric discipline depended partly on her adoption of its dominant theoretical framework.

---

### 4.4 Ellsworth Huntington — Climate, Civilisation, and Race

**Ellsworth Huntington** (1876–1947) — geographer at Yale University; prolific author of 28 books and over 240 articles — was the most extreme, the most explicitly racist, and the most methodologically ambitious of the major environmental determinists. His work represents the point at which environmental determinism converged most completely with **eugenics** — the pseudo-scientific program for improving human racial stock through selective breeding.

_**Civilization and Climate**_ (1915) — his major geographic work — argued that **climate**, specifically the combination of moderate temperature and high **weather variability** (the stimulus of seasonal and day-to-day weather fluctuation), was the primary determinant of human civilisational capacity. His evidence: a **"civilisation index"** constructed from the ratings of **leading citizens** of Europe and America who were asked to rank the world's nations by civilisational achievement. The result — with remarkable and deeply suspicious convenience — showed the highest civilisational capacity in the regions of Western Europe and northeastern North America where Huntington's informants themselves lived and worked.

> _"Perhaps the most important single factor in determining the geographic distribution of human energy and vitality is the effect of temperature on physical activity and mental alertness."_ — Huntington, _Civilization and Climate_, 1915, p. 9

_**The Character of Races**_ (1924) — moved explicitly from environmental to racial explanation, arguing that the geographic distribution of mental and physical capacity reflected the differential effects of environment acting over many generations on human hereditary constitution — an argument that dissolved the distinction between environmental and racial determinism.

Huntington was an active member of the **American Eugenics Society** (serving on its board), a contributor to the **Second International Eugenics Congress** (1921), and a correspondent of **Madison Grant** (author of _**The Passing of the Great Race**_, 1916 — one of the primary texts of American scientific racism). The intellectual relationship between environmental determinism and eugenics — between the argument that climate determined civilisational capacity and the argument that selective breeding could improve it — was not coincidental. Both were expressions of the same underlying conviction: that human inequality was natural, that its geographic distribution was scientifically explicable, and that this explanation justified both the political status quo and programs of active intervention to maintain or improve racial quality.

As **Livingstone** (_**The Geographical Tradition**_, 1992, Chapter 7) demonstrates, the relationship between geography and eugenics in the first three decades of the 20th century was not peripheral but structural: many of the leading figures of academic geography — including Huntington, **Henry Fairfield Osborn**, and others — were active participants in the eugenics movement, and the geographic and eugenic arguments were mutually reinforcing.

---

### 4.5 The Critique of Environmental Determinism — Possibilism

The **possibilist** critique of environmental determinism was developed primarily within the **French geographic tradition** associated with **Paul Vidal de la Blache** — though possibilism as a systematic position was named by the historian **Lucien Febvre** rather than by any geographer.

**Lucien Febvre** (1878–1956) — historian and co-founder (with **Marc Bloch**) of the **Annales school** of historical scholarship — coined the term **possibilism** in _**La Terre et l'Évolution Humaine: Introduction Géographique à l'Histoire**_ (_**A Geographical Introduction to History**_, 1922) to designate the French geographic tradition's alternative to determinism:

> _"There are no necessities, but everywhere possibilities; and man, as master of the possibilities, is the judge of their use."_ — Febvre, _A Geographical Introduction to History_ (translated by E.G. Mountford and J.H. Paxton, 1925), p. 236

This formulation — **"no necessities, but everywhere possibilities"** — is the canonical statement of possibilism and one of the most frequently quoted passages in the history of geographic thought. Its epistemological import: the physical environment determined nothing — it set a range of possibilities within which human agency, cultural tradition, and historical circumstance determined what actually occurred.

**Paul Vidal de la Blache** (1845–1918) — the intellectual founder of the tradition that Febvre labelled possibilism, though Vidal himself never used the term. As **Anne Buttimer** demonstrates in _**Society and Milieu in the French Geographic Tradition**_ (1971) — the definitive English-language study — Vidal's opposition to determinism was not primarily a theoretical argument but a **methodological commitment**: the insistence that geographic understanding required attention to the **specific, historical, culturally particular** ways in which different communities had adapted to and transformed their environments, rather than the derivation of cultural character from environmental conditions according to universal deterministic laws.

His key concepts — _**genre de vie**_ and _**pays**_ — already introduced in the Basics section — carried the anti-determinist argument:

_**Genre de vie**_ (way of life) — the concept that the relationship between a community and its environment was mediated by a historically evolved, culturally specific **ensemble of practices** — agricultural techniques, settlement forms, social organisations, material cultures — that could not be deduced from environmental conditions but had to be understood through historical and ethnographic research. The same physical environment could support multiple different _genres de vie_, demonstrating that environment did not determine but only constrained the range of possible adaptations.

_**Pays**_ — the small, distinctive region defined by the coherent character produced by the **long historical interaction** between physical setting and human occupance. The _pays_ was not an environmental type — it was a **historical product**: the materialisation of a specific _genre de vie_ in a specific landscape over centuries of mutual adaptation.

**Vidal's major geographic works**:

_**Tableau de la Géographie de la France**_ (1903) — discussed in the Basics section — the methodological and literary masterwork of the possibilist tradition.

_**Principes de Géographie Humaine**_ (_**Principles of Human Geography**_, 1922, posthumous, edited by **Emmanuel de Martonne**) — the systematic statement of Vidal's human geographic theory, published four years after his death.

_**Atlas de France**_ (1908) — a collaborative thematic atlas of France, combining physical and human geographic themes in integrated regional synthesis.

**The Vidalian school** — Vidal trained a generation of French geographers who carried the possibilist tradition through the first half of the 20th century:

**Albert Demangeon** (1872–1940) — _**La Picardie et les régions voisines**_ (1905); _**Les Îles Britanniques**_ (1927) — produced the definitive regional monographs of the Vidalian tradition.

**Jean Brunhes** (1869–1930) — _**La Géographie Humaine**_ (1910; English trans. _**Human Geography**_, 1920) — systematised Vidal's human geography into a comprehensive descriptive science of human occupation of Earth's surface, organised around **essential facts of human geography**: houses and settlements, roads and transportation networks, fields and cultivated land, mines and animal exploitation, and destructive facts (clearing, dissipation, devastation).

**Camille Vallaux** (1870–1945) — extended Vidalian method to political geography and the geography of the sea.

**Emmanuel de Martonne** (1873–1955) — _**Traité de Géographie Physique**_ (_**Treatise of Physical Geography**_, 1909) — synthesised physical geography on Vidalian principles, maintaining the physical-human integration that Vidal had insisted upon.

---

### 4.6 Why Possibilism Was Insufficient — A Critical Assessment

The possibilist critique dismantled environmental determinism's logical structure — demonstrating that environment set conditions of possibility rather than outcomes, and that cultural agency operated within environmental constraints rather than being determined by them. But possibilism had its own significant limitations that postgraduate analysis must acknowledge:

**The under-theorisation of power** — possibilism replaced environmental determination with cultural agency, but it had no adequate theory of the social relations within which cultural agency operated. Who in a given society had the agency to make geographic choices? How were the benefits and costs of different _genres de vie_ distributed between social groups? Vidal's possibilism could describe the diversity of human geographic practice but could not explain its **political economy** — the relations of class, gender, and colonial power that determined who benefited from geographic arrangements and who bore their costs.

**The romanticisation of the _pays_** — the Vidalian tradition's focus on small, distinctive, historically stable regions — the French _pays_ with their centuries-old _genres de vie_ — was implicitly **nostalgic** and **conservative**: it celebrated the traditional over the modern, the particular over the universal, the local over the global. As **Harvey** (_Social Justice and the City_, 1973) would later argue, this romanticism was politically disabling: it celebrated geographic diversity while ignoring the capitalist processes that were destroying it.

**The methodological nationalism** — Vidalian geography was overwhelmingly focused on France and Western Europe. Its conceptual vocabulary — _pays_, _genre de vie_, _circulation_ — was developed from and for the analysis of French regional geography. Its application to colonial territories — where the _genre de vie_ of indigenous peoples had been violently disrupted by colonial power — was either absent or unreflective.

**The physical-human balance** — while Vidal insisted on the importance of both physical and human factors, his methodology weighted the **descriptive-historical** over the **analytical-physical**. The physical geographic foundation of his regional monographs was often thin — providing the setting for human occupance rather than the substance of geographic analysis.

## PART V — POLITICAL GEOGRAPHY AND CLASSICAL GEOPOLITICS

### 5.1 The Emergence of Political Geography as a Formal Sub-Discipline

**Political geography** — the systematic study of the geographic dimensions of political power, territorial organisation, and international relations — emerged as a formal academic sub-discipline during Phase II, driven by the convergence of Ratzlian anthropogeography, European imperial competition, and the development of the modern nation-state system.

Its institutionalisation as a distinct sub-field is conventionally dated to Ratzel's _**Politische Geographie**_ (1897), though its intellectual roots extended through **Montesquieu** and **Ritter** and its practical applications were as old as the strategic geographic intelligence of the Roman Empire.

---

### 5.2 Halford Mackinder — Heartland Theory and the Geographic Pivot

**Sir Halford John Mackinder** (1861–1947) — geographer, politician (Liberal Unionist MP for Glasgow Camlachie, 1910–1922), colonial administrator (British High Commissioner for South Russia, 1919–1920), and first Reader in Geography at Oxford University (1887) — was the most influential geopolitical thinker of Phase II and arguably the most influential in the discipline's history.

His geopolitical significance rests on three texts, each marking a distinct phase in the development of his theoretical framework:

_**"The Geographical Pivot of History"**_ (_**Geographical Journal**_, 23(4), 1904, pp. 421–437) — the founding text of classical geopolitics, delivered as a paper to the **Royal Geographical Society** on January 25, 1904. Mackinder's central argument:

The **Columbian era** — the four centuries of European global expansion enabled by maritime power — was ending. The completion of the global railway network meant that the vast interior of the Eurasian landmass — previously inaccessible and therefore unable to project power outward — could now be organised and mobilised. The **geographic pivot of history** was the **Eurasian Heartland** (which he initially called the **Pivot Area**): the vast, resource-rich interior of Russia and Central Asia, drained by rivers flowing into the Arctic or into enclosed seas (the Volga to the Caspian), and therefore inaccessible to sea power. He argued:

> _"Who rules East Europe commands the Heartland; Who rules the Heartland commands the World-Island; Who rules the World-Island commands the World."_ — Mackinder, _Democratic Ideals and Reality_, 1919, p. 150

This formulation — the most quoted passage in the history of geopolitics — appeared not in the 1904 paper but in its major theoretical elaboration:

_**Democratic Ideals and Reality: A Study in the Politics of Reconstruction**_ (1919) — written during the Paris Peace Conference negotiations and directed at the peacemakers attempting to construct a stable post-war international order. Mackinder refined his concept of the Pivot Area into the **Heartland** (the interior of Eurasia, roughly corresponding to the territory of the Soviet Union), identified the **World-Island** (the combined landmass of Eurasia and Africa), and argued that the Heartland — protected from sea power, containing enormous resources, and increasingly accessible to land power through railways — was the geographic key to global dominance. Britain's strategic objective, in Mackinder's framework, was to prevent any single power from controlling the Heartland — hence his support for the independence of the East European **buffer states** (Poland, Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia) as geographic barriers between Germany and Russia.

_**"The Round World and the Winning of the Peace"**_ (_**Foreign Affairs**_, 21(4), 1943, pp. 595–605) — a final revision of the Heartland concept, written during the Second World War, in which Mackinder added the concept of the **Midland Ocean** (the North Atlantic) as a potential counter-balance to Heartland power — acknowledging that the United States, Britain, and France constituted a maritime alliance capable of projecting sufficient power to balance a potential Heartland hegemon.

**The intellectual genealogy of the Heartland theory**:

Mackinder drew on several intellectual traditions:

**Ritter's continental morphology** — the argument that the shape and structure of continents shaped their historical trajectories

**Ratzel's political geography** — particularly the concepts of territorial power and geopolitical competition

**The naval historian Alfred Thayer Mahan's** analysis of sea power — which Mackinder was explicitly contesting: Mahan (_**The Influence of Sea Power upon History, 1660–1783**_, 1890) had argued for the decisive importance of naval power; Mackinder argued that the railway was transferring the advantage from sea to land power

**Social Darwinist geopolitics** — the late 19th century intellectual climate in which international relations were understood as a Darwinian competition between states for geographic resources and strategic position

**The reception and legacy of Heartland theory**:

The Heartland theory was received differently in different national contexts and at different historical moments. In **Germany**, it was absorbed — through **Karl Haushofer's** _Geopolitik_ school at Munich — into the intellectual framework of Nazi strategic planning: **Rudolf Hess** attended Haushofer's seminars; Haushofer's concept of _**Lebensraum**_ (derived from Ratzel) combined with Mackinder's Heartland to produce a geopolitical justification for German Eastern expansion. As **Gearóid Ó Tuathail** (_**Critical Geopolitics**_, 1996, Chapter 2) demonstrates, the relationship between Mackinder's academic geopolitics and Nazi Geopolitik was mediated by Haushofer's selective and distorting appropriation rather than direct intellectual transmission — but the structural affinity between the frameworks was real.

In the **United States**, Heartland theory was revived by **Nicholas Spykman** (1893–1943) — _**America's Strategy in World Politics**_ (1942); _**The Geography of the Peace**_ (1944) — who argued, against Mackinder, that it was the **Rimland** (the coastal states surrounding the Heartland — Western Europe, the Middle East, South and East Asia) rather than the Heartland itself that was geographically decisive: _"Who controls the Rimland rules Eurasia; who rules Eurasia controls the destinies of the world"_ (_**The Geography of the Peace**_, 1944, p. 43). Spykman's Rimland theory directly influenced **George Kennan's** policy of **containment** and shaped American Cold War strategy — the system of alliances (NATO, SEATO, CENTO) designed to contain Soviet power by maintaining American control of the Rimland.

In contemporary strategic discourse, Mackinder's framework is explicitly invoked in analyses of **Chinese strategic geography**: the **Belt and Road Initiative**, interpreted as a Chinese attempt to develop continental infrastructure connecting China to Central Asia and Europe, has been described by multiple strategic analysts as a Heartland strategy — an attempt to organise and mobilise the resources of the Eurasian interior in ways that Mackinder feared. As **Bruno Maçães** argues in _**Belt and Road: A Chinese World Order**_ (2018), whether or not this characterisation is accurate, the **Mackinderian vocabulary** — Heartland, World-Island, rimland — continues to structure strategic geographic discourse more than a century after its formulation.

**Critical assessment of Mackinder**:

**Ó Tuathail's** critical geopolitics framework identifies three dimensions of Mackinder's geopolitical discourse that require critical deconstruction:

First, **the naturalisation of geographic determinism**: Mackinder presented his geopolitical framework as the objective reading of geographic facts — the natural strategic logic of Earth's physical configuration — while suppressing the extent to which his framework reflected the specific strategic interests of the British Empire at a particular historical moment.

Second, **the construction of strategic space**: the Heartland was not a pre-given geographic fact that Mackinder discovered — it was a **geographic construction** that he produced through specific choices about what geographic features to emphasise (river drainage, railway accessibility, resource endowment) and what to ignore (political organisation, cultural diversity, economic development).

Third, **the geopolitical text as political argument**: _Democratic Ideals and Reality_ was explicitly political — written to influence the Paris Peace Conference — and its geographic analysis was shaped by its political purpose. The Heartland theory was not a neutral geographic description but a **political argument dressed in geographic language** — a characteristic of geopolitical discourse that **Ó Tuathail** identified as its defining feature.

---

### 5.3 Alfred Thayer Mahan — Sea Power and Geographic Strategy

**Alfred Thayer Mahan** (1840–1914) — Rear Admiral in the United States Navy, President of the Naval War College (1886–1889, 1892–1893) — provided the sea-power counterpart to Mackinder's Heartland theory and was, arguably, even more directly influential on the strategic decisions of the major powers in the decade before the First World War.

_**The Influence of Sea Power upon History, 1660–1783**_ (1890) — argued that national greatness and imperial power depended primarily on **commercial and naval control of the seas**. Mahan identified six **geographic conditions** that determined national capacity for sea power:

1. **Geographic position** — access to the sea and freedom from land-based threats
2. **Physical conformation** — the character of the coastline (harbours, navigable rivers)
3. **Extent of territory** — the length of coastline relative to population and economic capacity
4. **Population size** — the labour force available for maritime industries and naval manning
5. **Character of the people** — commercial aptitude and maritime tradition
6. **Character of the government** — political support for naval and commercial maritime development

The framework was explicitly geographic — it identified the **spatial conditions** for sea power — but its political prescriptions were clear: the United States should build a powerful blue-water navy, acquire overseas bases (particularly in the Caribbean and Pacific), construct a canal across Central America (the future Panama Canal, completed 1914), and project naval power to maintain freedom of commerce in the key maritime lanes.

Mahan's influence was immediate and global: his book was read by **Kaiser Wilhelm II**, who ordered copies distributed to the German naval officers; it influenced **Theodore Roosevelt's** naval build-up; it shaped **Admiral Tirpitz's** construction of the German High Seas Fleet; and it was studied carefully by the **Japanese naval command** that planned the attack on the Russian fleet at Port Arthur (1904). As **Philip Crowl** argues in _"Alfred Thayer Mahan: The Naval Historian"_ (in **Peter Paret**, ed., _**Makers of Modern Strategy**_, 1986), Mahan was the most influential strategic writer of the late 19th century — and his influence was exercised through geographic argument.

---

### 5.4 The Institutionalisation of Geography and its Imperial Entanglements

Phase II was the period of geography's **institutional consolidation** as a formal academic discipline — and that consolidation was inseparable from the structures of European imperial power.

**The Royal Geographical Society** (RGS, founded 1830) was the most important institutional node of British Phase II geography. Its founding purposes were explicitly exploration and imperial intelligence: the support of expeditions to **Africa**, **Asia**, **Australia**, and the **Arctic**; the training of military and colonial officers in geographic method; the publication of geographic research through its _**Journal**_ (from 1831) and _**Proceedings**_ (from 1855, subsequently merged with the _Journal_ in 1893). The RGS funded **David Livingstone's** Central African expeditions, **Henry Morton Stanley's** Congo exploration, and dozens of other imperial expeditions whose geographic products — charts, surveys, natural history collections, ethnographic descriptions — fed directly into the colonial administration of the territories explored.

**Felix Driver's** _**Geography Militant: Cultures of Exploration and Empire**_ (2001) is the definitive study of the relationship between the RGS, Victorian exploration, and imperial power. Driver demonstrates that the geography practised under RGS auspices was not simply the neutral collection of geographic knowledge — it was the production of **geographic intelligence** for imperial purposes, shaped by the strategic and commercial interests of the British Empire and embedded in the social relations of colonial power.

The **American Geographical Society** (AGS, founded 1851), the **National Geographic Society** (NGS, founded 1888), and the **Société de Géographie** (Paris, founded 1821) performed analogous institutional functions in their respective national contexts. The NGS's development of its magazine (from 1888) into a mass-circulation publication with a global readership deserves particular attention: by the early 20th century, _National Geographic_ was the primary vehicle through which geographic knowledge about the non-Western world was transmitted to the American public — and its representation of non-Western peoples (the famous bare-breasted indigenous women, the exoticised tribal cultures) reproduced, in popular form, the colonial geographic imagination that academic environmental determinism provided in scholarly form.

**Isaiah Bowman** (1878–1950) — Director of the American Geographical Society (1915–1935), chief geographic advisor to **Woodrow Wilson** at the Paris Peace Conference (1919), and President of the Johns Hopkins University (1935–1948) — was the most politically powerful American geographer of the 20th century. His _**The New World: Problems in Political Geography**_ (1921) — a comprehensive regional geography of the post-war world organised around the political-geographic problems created by the redrawing of boundaries at Paris — was widely read by policymakers, diplomats, and the educated public.

As **Neil Smith** demonstrates in _**American Empire: Roosevelt's Geographer and the Prelude to Globalization**_ (2003) — the most comprehensive study of Bowman's career — his geographic work was inseparable from his political role: his boundary recommendations at Paris, his Cold War advisory role under **Franklin Roosevelt**, and his involvement in post-war international planning were all exercises in what he explicitly called **applied geography** — the use of geographic knowledge in the service of state power.

## PART VI — THE PHYSICAL GEOGRAPHY OF PHASE II

### 6.1 The Development of Physical Geography as Systematic Science

While human geography in Phase II was primarily engaged with the debates between environmental determinism and possibilism, physical geography was undergoing its own **systematic scientific development** — establishing the theoretical frameworks and empirical standards that would define its practice through the 20th century.

The key development was the emergence of **process geomorphology** — the systematic study of the physical processes (weathering, erosion, deposition, tectonic activity) that shaped Earth's surface — replacing the earlier tradition of descriptive landscape classification with a theoretically grounded, process-based approach.

---

### 6.2 William Morris Davis and the Geographical Cycle

**William Morris Davis** (1850–1934) — geomorphologist at Harvard University; President of the Association of American Geographers (1904, 1905, 1909); the dominant figure in American physical geography for four decades — developed the most influential theoretical model in Phase II physical geography: the **geographical cycle** (also called the **cycle of erosion** or **davisian cycle**).

The model, developed through a series of papers from 1884 onward and synthesised in _**Geographical Essays**_ (1909), proposed that landscapes evolved through a predictable temporal sequence of **stages** following tectonic uplift:

**Youth** — rapid incision by streams, V-shaped valleys, high relief, minimal floodplain development, waterfalls and rapids abundant

**Maturity** — widening valleys, interlocking spurs eliminated, tributaries extended, maximum topographic relief, well-integrated drainage networks

**Old age** — subdued relief, wide floodplains, meandering rivers, approach to the **peneplain** (_from Latin pene_ — almost; _planum_ — plain): a nearly flat surface of low relief close to sea level, the theoretical endpoint of complete erosion

Tectonic **rejuvenation** — uplift of the peneplain restarted the cycle, producing **polycyclic landscapes** with multiple generations of erosion surfaces preserved at different altitudes.

Davis presented the geographical cycle through a specific literary and visual language: the landscape was **young**, **mature**, or **old**, passing through its life stages like a living organism. This **organic metaphor** — landscapes aging like bodies — was not merely rhetorical. It reflected Davis's theoretical commitment to an **evolutionary** framework derived from **Darwinian biology**, transposed from biological to geomorphological systems.

**The strengths of the Davisian cycle**:

- It provided a **unified theoretical framework** for understanding landscape evolution that was teachable, visually intuitive, and organised apparently diverse landforms into a coherent system
- It generated **testable predictions**: uplifted peneplain surfaces should be identifiable in high-altitude erosion surfaces; polycyclic landscapes should show systematic relationships between surface altitude and degree of dissection
- It connected **geomorphology to geology** — the cycle's stages corresponded to geological time periods, linking landform analysis to stratigraphic evidence

**The limitations of the Davisian cycle**:

**Theoretical** — the cycle was based on **deductive reasoning** from first principles rather than empirical measurement of actual erosion rates and processes. Davis rarely measured — he inferred. As **Keith Richards** demonstrates in _**Rivers: Form and Process in Alluvial Channels**_ (1982, Chapter 1), the cycle's predictions about the timing of stage transitions were based on qualitative inference rather than quantitative process understanding.

**Empirical** — the cycle required a specific sequence: rapid uplift followed by long stability during which erosion proceeded. In practice, **tectonic activity was continuous** rather than episodic; **climate changed** during the course of landscape evolution, altering the dominant processes; **rock type variation** produced landscapes that did not conform to the cycle's predictions. The **peneplain** — the theoretical endpoint — was rarely identified unambiguously in real landscapes.

**The Penck critique** — **Walther Penck** (1888–1923), German geomorphologist, provided the most theoretically sophisticated challenge to Davis in _**Die Morphologische Analyse**_ (_**The Morphological Analysis of Landforms**_, 1924, posthumous). Penck argued that slope form was determined by the **relative rates of uplift and erosion** rather than by the elapsed time since uplift. A slope's convexity or concavity reflected the dynamic balance between tectonic and erosional processes operating simultaneously — not a stage in a temporal sequence of progressively declining energy. This was a more physically realistic framework than Davis's, but its mathematical complexity and its unfortunate translation into English (which introduced additional confusion) limited its influence in the anglophone tradition until the mid-20th century.

---

### 6.3 Grove Karl Gilbert and Process Geomorphology

**Grove Karl Gilbert** (1843–1918) — geologist with the US Geological Survey and the most rigorous physical geographer of Phase II — represented a methodological alternative to Davis that proved, in retrospect, to be more scientifically productive.

Where Davis theorised landscape evolution through temporal stages derived from qualitative reasoning, Gilbert studied **specific geomorphological processes** through careful field measurement and the application of physical principles. His major works:

_**Report on the Geology of the Henry Mountains**_ (1877) — the first systematic analysis of **stream erosion** as a physical process: the relationship between stream velocity, discharge, gradient, and sediment load expressed in quantitative terms. Gilbert's concept of **dynamic equilibrium** — the tendency of stream systems to adjust their form toward a balance between energy supply (discharge and gradient) and energy expenditure (transporting the sediment load) — was a **process-based**, physically grounded alternative to Davis's stage-based, historically organised framework.

_**Lake Bonneville**_ (1890) — the most comprehensive geological study of a Pleistocene lake, demonstrating through detailed stratigraphic and geomorphological analysis the geographic consequences of the late Pleistocene and Holocene climate changes that had alternately filled and drained the Great Basin of the American West.

_**Hydraulic Mining Debris in the Sierra Nevada**_ (1917) — an early study of **human-induced geomorphological change**: the transformation of river channels and sedimentation patterns by the debris from hydraulic gold mining in the Sierra Nevada foothills, demonstrating quantitatively the geographic consequences of mining-induced sediment release on floodplain morphology and agricultural land.

As **Victor Baker** and **Garnett Williams** argue in _"Fluvial Geomorphology"_ (_**Geological Society of America Bulletin**_, 1980), Gilbert's approach — **quantitative, process-based, grounded in physical principles** — was the methodological foundation of the **quantitative revolution in geomorphology** that would occur in the 1950s–1960s. Davis was the more famous and the more institutionally influential figure of Phase II; Gilbert was the more scientifically productive.

---

### 6.4 Biogeography and the Darwinian Revolution

The publication of **Charles Darwin's** _**On the Origin of Species**_ (1859) — which, as noted, was intellectually indebted to Humboldtian geography — transformed biogeography from a descriptive science of geographic distribution into an **evolutionary science** seeking to explain distributions through the history of speciation, extinction, and dispersal.

**Alfred Russel Wallace** (1823–1913) — who had independently arrived at the theory of natural selection while conducting biogeographic research in the Malay Archipelago, and who had communicated his results to Darwin in the letter that precipitated the joint presentation to the Linnean Society in 1858 — produced the first systematic synthesis of evolutionary biogeography:

_**The Geographical Distribution of Animals: With a Study of the Relations of Living and Extinct Faunas as Elucidating the Past Changes of the Earth's Surface**_ (2 vols., 1876) — the foundational text of zoogeography. Wallace divided Earth's land surface into six **biogeographic realms** (Palaearctic, Nearctic, Neotropical, Ethiopian, Oriental, Australian) based on the distribution of mammalian families — identifying the major barriers to dispersal that had isolated evolutionary lineages and produced the characteristic fauna of each realm.

The **Wallace Line** — the biogeographic boundary between the Oriental and Australian realms, running between Bali and Lombok, between Borneo and Sulawesi — was identified by Wallace as the most sharp and dramatic biogeographic boundary on Earth: on its western side, the fauna was predominantly Asian (placental mammals, Asian birds); on its eastern side, the fauna was predominantly Australian (marsupials, monotremes, Australian birds). As **Mark Lomolino** et al. (_**Biogeography**_, 4th ed., 2010) document, the Wallace Line reflects the deep geological history of Southeast Asia: the islands west of the line sit on the Sunda Shelf (a shallow-water extension of the Asian continental shelf exposed during glacial sea-level lowstands), while those east of the line are separated from Australia by deeper water — a geographic barrier that has prevented faunal interchange for tens of millions of years.

_**Island Life**_ (1880) — Wallace's analysis of **island biogeography** — the characteristic fauna and flora of oceanic islands and the processes (dispersal, evolution, extinction) that had produced them. This work anticipated **MacArthur** and **Wilson's** equilibrium theory by 87 years and established the systematic questions that island biogeography would address for the next century.

---

### 6.5 Climatology in Phase II — Köppens Classification and the Atmosphere as Geographic System

**Wladimir Köppen** (1846–1940) — German-Russian climatologist and meteorologist — produced the most widely used climate classification system in geographic history, first published in 1884 and revised in major editions in 1918 and 1936 (with **Rudolf Geiger**, _**Klimakarte der Erde**_, 1954).

The **Köppen classification** — discussed in outline in the Basics section — was significant in Phase II not merely as a taxonomic system but as a **geographic theory**: the explicit claim that climate zones corresponded to vegetation zones, and that both could be mapped using the same set of temperature and precipitation thresholds. This correspondence — which Köppen demonstrated empirically — was a Humboldtian argument: it demonstrated that the atmosphere and the biosphere were systematically interconnected, that geographic patterns in one domain were reflected in geographic patterns in the other.

**Vladimir Koppen's** intellectual relationship with **Alfred Wegener** (1880–1930) — who was his son-in-law — deserves attention at the postgraduate level. Wegener's _**Die Entstehung der Kontinente und Ozeane**_ (_**The Origin of Continents and Oceans**_, 1915, 4th ed. 1929) — the systematic statement of **continental drift theory** — was the most important geological hypothesis of Phase II and the most violently contested.

Wegener argued, from evidence combining **paleoclimatological** (ancient climate indicators — coal deposits, glacial striae, fossil distributions — that made sense only if the continents had formerly been in different positions), **biogeographic** (the distribution of identical fossil species on now-separated continents), **geodetic** (measurements suggesting that Greenland was drifting westward relative to Europe), and **geometric** (the apparent fit of continental coastlines — particularly South America and Africa) that the continents had formerly been assembled in a single supercontinent (**Pangaea**) and had subsequently drifted apart.

The scientific reception of continental drift was predominantly **hostile** throughout Wegener's lifetime and for two decades after his death (he died during a meteorological expedition to Greenland in 1930). The primary objection was **mechanistic**: Wegener could not identify a physical mechanism capable of moving continents through the oceanic crust. It was not until **Harry Hess's** discovery of **seafloor spreading** (_"History of Ocean Basins"_, in _**Petrologic Studies: A Volume in Honor of A.F. Buddington**_, 1962) and the development of **plate tectonics** in the 1960s that the mechanism was identified and continental drift — transformed into plate tectonics — was accepted as the foundational framework of geology and physical geography.

The history of the reception of continental drift theory is instructive for the sociology of geographic knowledge: a framework that was, in retrospect, overwhelmingly correct in its empirical claims was resisted for decades by the geological and geophysical mainstream, primarily because it challenged the institutional consensus and because its mechanism was unknown. As **Naomi Oreskes** demonstrates in _**The Rejection of Continental Drift: Theory and Method in American Earth Science**_ (1999), the sociology of disciplinary authority — the power of established institutional frameworks to resist empirical challenges — is as important a determinant of the acceptance of geographic theories as their evidential support.

## PART VII — THE DISSOLUTION OF ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINISM

### 7.1 The Empirical Critique

Environmental determinism began dissolving as a credible scientific framework from the 1920s onward under the combined pressure of empirical, theoretical, and political critiques.

**The empirical critique** was the most straightforward: the theory's predictions were simply not supported by the evidence. Environmental determinism predicted that:

- Climate zones should correspond systematically to levels of civilisational achievement
- Peoples inhabiting the same climate zone should show similar cultural and intellectual characteristics
- The historical trajectory of civilisations should reflect their environmental positioning

All three predictions were extensively refuted:

- The same climate zone contained societies of radically different levels of economic development and cultural achievement
- The climatic conditions of the contemporary centres of industrial capitalism (northwestern Europe, northeastern North America) were not distinctive — similar climates existed elsewhere without producing comparable economic development
- Historically, the centres of the highest civilisational achievement had shifted repeatedly without corresponding climate change — the cradles of civilisation (Mesopotamia, the Nile Valley, the Indus Valley) were in climate zones that environmental determinism classified as relatively unfavourable

**Carl Sauer's** cultural geography — by demonstrating the cultural-historical specificity of landscape transformation and the diversity of _genres de vie_ developed by different cultures in similar environments — provided the most sustained empirical critique from within geography.

---

### 7.2 The Theoretical Critique

**Carl O. Sauer's** _"The Morphology of Landscape"_ (1925) and **Lucien Febvre's** _**A Geographical Introduction to History**_ (1922) provided the theoretical framework for possibilist critique. But the more fundamental theoretical challenge came from beyond geography: the development of **cultural anthropology** as a discipline committed to the principle of **cultural relativism**.

**Franz Boas** (1858–1942) — the founder of American cultural anthropology — spent his career systematically dismantling the framework of evolutionary and geographic determinism that had structured 19th-century anthropology. His demonstration that **race**, **language**, and **culture** were independent variables — that there was no systematic correspondence between biological racial categories, linguistic groups, and cultural practices — directly refuted the assumption shared by environmental determinism and racial eugenics that physical characteristics (including the physical characteristics of populations adapted to specific climates) determined cultural capacity.

**Alfred Kroeber's** (1876–1960) concept of the **culture area** — regions defined by the distribution of shared cultural traits — was an explicitly **anti-determinist** geographic concept: culture areas did not correspond to environmental zones, demonstrating that cultural distributions were produced by cultural-historical processes (diffusion, borrowing, invention) rather than environmental determination.

---

### 7.3 The Political Critique — After Fascism

The **association of environmental determinism with fascism** was ultimately its intellectual death sentence in academic geography — though the timing of the critique, concentrated after 1945, raises the uncomfortable question of why the geographical mainstream had not more actively resisted the framework during its period of dominance.

The connections were multiple and specific:

- **Ratzel's** _Lebensraum_ concept was a direct intellectual input into Nazi territorial ideology
- **Haushofer's** _Geopolitik_ — which deployed Ratzlian and Mackinderian concepts in an explicitly Nazi-aligned strategic framework — was widely perceived in the Allied countries as the intellectual apparatus of German expansionism
- **Huntington's** active participation in the eugenics movement connected environmental determinism to the racial science that provided theoretical justification for Nazi racial policies

After 1945, these connections made environmental determinism politically toxic in the academic mainstream. The discipline's response — to abandon the framework without sustained critical reflection on why it had commanded assent for so long — was intellectually inadequate but institutionally understandable.

**Richard Peet's** _"The Social Origins of Environmental Determinism"_ (_**Annals of the Association of American Geographers**_, 1985) remains the most important critical analysis of why environmental determinism achieved the intellectual authority it did — arguing that its persistence reflected not its empirical adequacy but its ideological functionality: it provided geographic legitimation for the racial hierarchy on which European imperialism and American racial capitalism depended.

---

### 7.4 The Partial Revival — Contemporary Echoes

The intellectual death of environmental determinism as an academic doctrine has not prevented its core arguments from reappearing in modified forms in subsequent scholarship — a recurrence that demands critical attention at the postgraduate level.

**Jeffrey Sachs's** _**The End of Poverty**_ (2005) and his related papers (particularly _"Institutions Don't Rule: Direct Effects of Geography on Per Capita Income"_, _**NBER Working Paper**_, 2003) argued that tropical geography — specifically the disease burden of the tropics, the agricultural limitations of tropical soils, and the transport disadvantages of landlocked tropical countries — created genuine economic development disadvantages that persisted independently of institutional arrangements. This was not crude environmental determinism — Sachs acknowledged the role of institutions — but it attributed geographic factors a primary causal role in explaining income differences between nations.

**Daron Acemoglu** and **James A. Robinson's** _**Why Nations Fail: The Origins of Power, Prosperity, and Poverty**_ (2012) was substantially constructed as a rebuttal of Sachs's geographic argument: the central thesis — that **institutions** (specifically the distinction between **inclusive** and **extractive** political and economic institutions) rather than geography explained the wealth and poverty of nations — was argued against the geographic determinism that Sachs represented.

The Sachs-Acemoglu-Robinson debate is the most important contemporary echo of the Phase II determinist-possibilist controversy, and it demonstrates that the intellectual questions raised by environmental determinism — what role does physical geography play in determining economic development outcomes? — remain genuinely open rather than settled. What has been settled — permanently, by the empirical critique, the theoretical critique, and the political critique — is the specific determinist answer: the claim that physical environment directly determines cultural capacity. But the broader question of the relationship between physical geographic conditions and human social outcomes remains one of the most important in the discipline.

## PART VIII — THE LEGACY OF PHASE II FOR CONTEMPORARY GEOGRAPHY

### 8.1 The Permanent Contributions

Phase II established several **permanent contributions** to geographic thought that subsequent phases have built on rather than superseded:

**The Humboldtian synthesis** — the concept of Earth as an integrated system of interconnected physical and human processes — is the most important theoretical legacy of Phase II. Its influence on contemporary **Earth system science**, **political ecology**, **climate geography**, and **more-than-human geography** is direct and acknowledged. **Humboldt's name** is attached to the cold Pacific coastal current (**Humboldt Current** or **Peru Current**), to a species of penguin (_Spheniscus humboldti_), to multiple geographic features across the Americas, and — with maximum symbolic fitness — to the **Humboldtian AI assistant** that inherits his commitment to synthesis across domains of knowledge.

**Process geomorphology** — Gilbert's process-based approach to landscape analysis, combined with the quantitative methods of Phase IV, has produced the most scientifically rigorous tradition in physical geography. Contemporary **fluvial geomorphology**, **hillslope hydrology**, and **tectonic geomorphology** are direct descendants of Gilbert's Phase II work.

**The geopolitical tradition** — Mackinder's Heartland theory, Mahan's sea-power thesis, and Ratzel's political geography established a vocabulary and a set of analytical frameworks — territorial competition, strategic resources, geographic pivots, balance of power — that continues to structure strategic geographic discourse. The frameworks are contested, theoretically problematic, and historically compromised — but they remain the primary conceptual vocabulary of applied political geography.

**The cartographic revolution** — Mercator's projection, the atlas format, and the institutionalisation of standardised cartographic conventions established the representational infrastructure of geographic knowledge that persisted into the digital era. Critical cartography's challenge to this infrastructure — Harley's deconstruction of the map — is only possible because the infrastructure is so thoroughly established.

---

### 8.2 The Unresolved Problems

Phase II also bequeathed several **unresolved problems** that continue to structure geographic thought:

**The nature-society relationship** — Phase II's central theoretical dispute — between environmental determinism (nature determines society) and possibilism (society has agency within natural constraints) — has not been resolved by subsequent phases. It has been reframed — through Marxist political economy (society produces nature), poststructuralism (nature is a social construction), and more-than-human geography (nature and society are mutually constitutive) — but the underlying question of how physical geographic processes and social processes interact to produce geographic outcomes remains the discipline's deepest theoretical problem.

**The colonial legacy of geographic knowledge** — Phase II's geographic institutions (the RGS, the AGS, the Société de Géographie), geographic practitioners (Mackinder, Bowman, the environmental determinists), and geographic texts (the colonial surveys, the regional geographic descriptions of colonised territories, the world maps produced under European cartographic conventions) were constitutively implicated in European imperial power. The **decolonial geography** movement — **Jazeel's** _Subaltern Geographies_, **McKittrick's** _Demonic Grounds_, the curriculum reform debates of the 2010s–2020s — is the contemporary effort to reckon with this legacy and reconstruct a geography that does not reproduce colonial epistemologies. Phase II is where that effort must begin, because it is where the colonial formation of academic geography was most thoroughly institutionalised.

**The geography-geopolitics relation** — the relationship between geographic analysis and strategic/political discourse remains structurally problematic. Mackinder's geopolitics was presented as objective geographic science while being simultaneously a political argument for specific strategic choices. Contemporary **critical geopolitics** — Ó Tuathail, Agnew, **Simon Dalby** — has developed the analytical tools for deconstructing geopolitical discourse. But the question of whether it is possible to produce geographic knowledge of strategic relevance without that knowledge being captured by the strategic interests it seeks to inform is not resolved.

_Phase II establishes the discipline's most contested inheritance: the Humboldtian vision of geographic synthesis on one side; the environmental determinist tradition of geographic ideology on the other. Phase III — Regional Geography and Possibilism — represents the first systematic attempt to consolidate the former while dismantling the latter. Its success was partial, its failure instructive, and its legacy — the insistence on the geographic significance of the particular, the specific, the placed — remains one of the discipline's most important and most undervalued intellectual commitments._
